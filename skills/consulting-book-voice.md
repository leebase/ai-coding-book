# Skill: Consulting Book Voice

This skill defines the voice for a thesis-driven business book written for
consulting leaders, operators, and executive readers.

## Who You Are Writing To

Smart, busy readers who understand business pressure and delivery work. They do
not need hype. They need a sharper way to see what is changing and what to do
about it.

## The Register

Write like a reflective operator explaining something consequential to another
experienced operator.

- Human, not institutional
- Reflective, not bloodless
- Inviting, not performative
- Sharp, not breathless
- Strategic, not vague
- Practical, not procedural

The best comparison is not a consulting firm white paper. It is a seasoned
practitioner thinking clearly on the page.

## Core Voice Promise

The prose should feel like it was written by a person who has been in the room,
seen the trap, understands why smart people fall into it, and wants to help the
reader think better.

Authority should come from lived recognition and clear judgment, not from
institutional tone.

## What Good Prose Does Here

- Sounds like a person, not a brand
- Names the pressure clearly
- Acknowledges why the wrong move is tempting
- Makes the false move feel tempting before exposing it
- Uses stories as proof, not ornament
- Connects evidence to action
- Leaves the reader with a clearer decision to make

## Preferred Moves

- Use lived language over corporate language
- Let the reader feel that a real mind is working through the problem
- Favor phrases that could be spoken in conversation without sounding fake
- Use reflection to deepen the argument, not to soften it
- Make the economic point without flattening the human one

Examples of the right kind of move:

- "Of course leaders want the savings."
- "I understand why this looks like the right move."
- "The logic breaks when you take all the gains in one direction."
- "What disappears is rarely visible on the org chart."

## Human Stakes

This book is partly about strategy, but it is also about what gets lost when
organizations treat people as cost categories instead of capability carriers.

Let the human stakes stay present in the prose. Do not sermonize, but do not
write as if continuity, judgment, dignity, and underused talent are side
issues.

## Avoid

- White-paper throat clearing
- Trend-report language
- Generic management abstractions
- McKinsey-wannabe phrasing
- Slide-deck prose
- Consultant cliches like `reimagine`, `unlock value`, `navigate disruption`,
  and `transform at scale`
- Repeating the same claim without adding force
- Hero worship, including of AI

## Tone Check

Before accepting a passage, ask:

- Does this sound like Lee, or like a firm trying to sound important?
- Is there at least one sentence here that feels observed rather than assembled?
- Would a smart reader feel talked with instead of talked at?
- Did the prose preserve warmth without losing edge?
