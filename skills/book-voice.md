# Skill: Book Voice

This book has a specific voice. When writing any chapter prose, adopt it.

## Who You Are Writing To

A working developer. They have shipped code. They are not a beginner. They have been using AI coding tools — autocomplete, chat, maybe an agent — and they have noticed that "just prompting" does not reliably work. They suspect there is a reason. They are reading this book because they want to understand why and fix it.

They do not need to be impressed. They need to be shown.

## The Register

**Peer-to-peer, not professor-to-student.**

Write as a senior developer talking to another developer who is slightly behind on one specific thing. Not condescending. Not cheerleading. Just direct.

- Say "you" freely. This is a conversation.
- Use short sentences when the point is sharp. Expand only when the concept requires it.
- Name the frustration before you solve it. The reader is not coming in neutral — they have been burned by AI chaos. Acknowledge it.
- Do not hedge. If something is true, say it. If something is wrong, say that too.

## What This Book Argues

The book has a thesis. Every chapter serves it:

> Software engineering discipline does not disappear in the AI era. It moves up a level. The AI writes the code. You are responsible for everything the code is supposed to do.

Let this thesis surface naturally in the prose. Do not lecture it. Show the reader a situation where the discipline was absent and something broke, then show them the same situation with discipline applied.

## What to Avoid

- **Academic tone**: Do not write "it can be observed that" or "one might consider". Write "notice that" or "consider".
- **Cheerleading**: Do not tell the reader how exciting or powerful AI is. They know. They are here for the discipline, not the hype.
- **Padding**: Do not warm up to the point. Start with the point.
- **False balance**: This book has opinions. Do not hedge them into mush.
- **Jargon without payoff**: Every technical term earns its place or gets cut.

## Callout Boxes

Use sparingly. Reserve for:
- `> **Free Tier Note:**` — when rate limits or feature restrictions affect a step
- `> **What the Agent Will Do:**` — before autonomous Antigravity action, so the reader knows what to expect
- `> **Watch For:**` — when a specific output or behavior confirms success
- `> **Key Takeaway:**` — the one thing the reader must leave the chapter knowing

Do not use callouts to restate what was just said. Use them to add information the reader needs before continuing.

## Chapter Endings

Every chapter ends with one sentence. Not a summary. Not a list of what was covered. One sentence that names the principle the reader just experienced and points forward.

Example: "Now that you know what happens when you skip the plan, let's look at what a good one actually contains."
