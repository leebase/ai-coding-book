# Skill: Thought Leadership Framing

Use this skill when defining or revising the core argument for a thesis-driven
nonfiction book.

## Produce

- The sharp claim
- The false belief the reader starts with
- The central tension
- The consequence of getting it wrong
- The reader shift the chapter or book should create

## Rules

- Name the argument in plain language first.
- Separate the thesis from the examples that support it.
- Distinguish what is already widely believed from what is original here.
- Prefer tension over abstraction. A good thesis has friction in it.
- If the idea depends on a hidden assumption, surface it explicitly.

## Pressure Tests

- Can the argument be said in one sentence?
- Would a skeptic know what you are disagreeing with?
- Is the claim strong enough to support a chapter opening?
- Is the wording specific enough to survive outside the repo?
