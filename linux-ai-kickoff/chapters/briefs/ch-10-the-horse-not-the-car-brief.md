# Chapter 10 Brief - The Horse, Not the Car

## Mission

- Mission: Teach the Horse-Not-Car model so the reader understands that AI is
  powerful but not deterministic, and that guidance and correction are normal
  parts of using it well.
- Why it matters: After Chapter 9, the reader can choose the right helper and
  give it a mission. Now they need to understand why a well-aimed prompt still
  produces drift, overreach, and wrong turns sometimes.
- Success looks like: The reader can spot one example of drift, name what went
  wrong, and give one redirect that improves the interaction.
- Next door it opens: Chapter 11 can teach a fuller practical loop of ask,
  refine, test, and repeat once the reader no longer expects one-shot
  obedience.

## Purpose

This chapter needs to:

1. adapt Lee's horse metaphor into clean, practical prose
2. explain that AI behavior is not car-like or button-precise
3. normalize correction without making AI sound mystical
4. show a few common forms of drift in plain language
5. end with one small correction exercise the reader can do immediately

## Working Example Set

Primary drift examples:

- a practical project coach drifting into a giant plan
- a patient Linux teacher drifting into too much jargon

Core correction moves:

1. name the drift
2. restate the real goal
3. shrink the task
4. redirect the answer shape

Keep out of chapter:

- long debates about whether AI is "really" thinking
- technical model architecture explanation
- multi-turn workflow detail that belongs in Chapter 11
- prompt-engineering jargon
- turning the horse metaphor into a gimmick

## Working Outline

1. Why people expect car behavior from AI
2. Why horse is a better model
3. What drift looks like in practice
4. How to correct without panic
5. One worked correction example
6. One reader exercise and Chapter 11 handoff
