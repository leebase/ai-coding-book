# Chapter 8 Brief - AI Is Not a Vending Machine

## Mission

- Mission: Reframe AI from answer machine to tutor, coach, and collaborator,
  then give the reader one simple way to rewrite weak AI requests into useful
  ones.
- Why it matters: If the reader treats AI like a vending machine, they will
  keep asking vague questions and mistaking noisy output for failure or magic.
- Success looks like: The reader can take one weak AI request and rewrite it
  into a grounded, narrow prompt that asks for one kind of help.
- Next door it opens: Chapter 9 can teach the fuller Team-Captain model once
  the reader already understands that AI needs direction.

## Purpose

This chapter needs to:

1. explain why vending-machine thinking fails
2. keep the human clearly in charge
3. teach AI as explainer, comparer, and next-step helper
4. avoid stealing Chapter 9's full role/backstory/mission framework
5. end with a visible prompt rewrite exercise

## Working Example Set

Anchor examples:

- weak: `How do I use Linux better?`
- stronger: `I'm on Omarchy. I already read the Shell Tools section. Explain ff, fd, and rg in plain English and tell me which one to try first.`
- weak: `Teach me coding`
- stronger: `Give me the next smallest step to build a useful tool for my launchpad project.`

Core chapter move:

- teach the reader to improve a prompt by:
  1. grounding it in the real situation
  2. narrowing it to one question
  3. asking for one kind of help

Keep out of chapter:

- role prompting as a full framework
- the executive/team-captain metaphor in full
- the horse-not-car metaphor in full
- long prompt-engineering taxonomies
- advanced AI-harness setup

## Working Outline

1. The vending-machine mistake
2. What AI is actually good at when used well
3. Three kinds of help to ask for: explain, compare, next step
4. Weak prompt vs grounded prompt examples
5. One simple rewrite pattern
6. End with a reader prompt rewrite exercise
