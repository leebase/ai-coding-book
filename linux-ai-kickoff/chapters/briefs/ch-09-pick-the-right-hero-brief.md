# Chapter 9 Brief - Pick the Right Hero

## Mission

- Mission: Teach the Team-Captain model directly so the reader can choose the
  right kind of AI helper, give it useful context, assign a concrete mission,
  and stay in charge of the interaction.
- Why it matters: Once the reader stops treating AI like a vending machine,
  the next upgrade is learning how to direct it on purpose instead of asking
  the void for help.
- Success looks like: The reader builds one role prompt for a real task using
  hero, backstory, mission, and stay-in-charge guidance.
- Next door it opens: Chapter 10 can explain why even a well-chosen helper
  still drifts and needs correction.

## Purpose

This chapter needs to:

1. name the Team-Captain model clearly
2. adapt the executive model into reader-native language
3. show why the same AI gives different value based on the helper chosen
4. teach the four-part framework without drowning in prompt jargon
5. end with one real role prompt the reader can keep

## Working Example Set

Anchor helpers:

- `patient Linux teacher`
- `practical project coach`
- `careful explainer`

Core framework:

1. Pick the right hero
2. Give them the backstory
3. Give them a specific mission
4. Stay in charge

Keep out of chapter:

- multi-agent orchestration
- long persona catalogs
- advanced system prompts
- the horse-not-car metaphor in full
- complicated prompting syntax games

## Working Outline

1. Why "the AI" is too vague
2. Same machine, different helpers
3. The Team-Captain model
4. Hero, backstory, mission, stay in charge
5. Weak prompt vs directed role prompt
6. End with one role prompt the reader can keep
