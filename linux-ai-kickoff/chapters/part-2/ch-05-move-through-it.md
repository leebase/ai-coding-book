# Move Through It

Meeting the machine is one thing.

Moving through it is another.

That difference matters.

You can know how to open the launcher and still feel hesitant every time you
need to do something simple.

That hesitation adds up.

It makes the system feel more complicated than it is.

It makes every small task feel like you might lose your place.

And once that feeling shows up, people start reaching for the mouse, random
clicking, or backing away from the machine altogether.

We are going to fix that now.

Not by teaching every hotkey.

By teaching a few movement patterns that you will use over and over.

## Movement Is Confidence

One of the quiet differences between a person who feels at home in a system and
someone who feels lost is movement.

The person who feels at home can get where they need to go without building a
whole emotional event around it.

Open this.

Switch there.

Close that.

Paste this.

Go back.

That smoothness is not magic.

It comes from a small set of repeated actions becoming familiar.

That is what this chapter is for.

You are not trying to become impressive.

You are trying to become less hesitant.

That is a better goal.

## Workspaces Are Rooms

The Omarchy manual's `Hotkeys` section gives you several ways to move between
workspaces.

Think of workspaces like rooms.

You do not need to understand every possible way to arrange the house.

You just need to know how to move between the rooms you are actually using.

Start with these:

- `Super + Tab` for the next workspace
- `Super + Shift + Tab` for the previous workspace
- `Super + 1/2/3/4` to jump directly to a specific workspace

That is enough to start.

Use them once or twice and notice what changes.

The machine feels less like one crowded surface.

You start to get separation.

One room for browsing.

One room for terminal work.

One room for notes.

One room for whatever else you need.

That separation lowers mental noise.

## Learn Two Simple Window Controls

Now take two more useful controls from the `Hotkeys` section.

Close a window:

`Super + W`

Toggle full screen:

`Super + F`

That is it.

Again, the point is not mastery.

The point is relief.

If you can move around, close what you are done with, and give one thing full
attention when you need it, the system starts to feel workable instead of busy.

That matters more than a giant list of controls ever will.

## The Clipboard Should Not Fight You

One of the smartest quality-of-life ideas in the Omarchy manual is the unified
clipboard.

Linux often makes copy and paste feel inconsistent between apps and the
terminal.

That is exactly the kind of small friction that wears people down.

Omarchy smooths that out with a simpler pattern:

- `Super + C` to copy
- `Super + X` to cut
- `Super + V` to paste

And if you want clipboard history:

- `Super + Ctrl + V`

The manual notes that this works almost everywhere, with exceptions like the
file manager and some AI agent CLIs.

That is worth naming because it keeps the reader from blaming themselves when a
system is not perfectly uniform.

This is a good example of how the machine is helping you.

It is removing one annoying category of friction so your attention can stay on
the work.

## Clipboard History Is A Bigger Deal Than It Sounds

The clipboard history feature matters because it saves you from one of the most
common tiny annoyances in digital work:

copying one thing, then losing it because you copied something else.

With `Super + Ctrl + V`, you can bring up clipboard history, choose the thing
you want, and then paste it with `Super + V`.

That sounds small.

It is small.

That is exactly why it matters.

Good systems remove repeated small annoyances.

When they do that well enough, your day feels lighter.

That is what leverage often looks like in real life.

Not fireworks.

Less drag.

## What To Practice Right Now

Do this short drill once or twice:

1. Open the browser
2. Move to the next workspace with `Super + Tab`
3. Open a terminal
4. Jump back with `Super + Shift + Tab`
5. Copy a short line of text with `Super + C`
6. Paste it somewhere else with `Super + V`
7. Open clipboard history with `Super + Ctrl + V`
8. Close a window with `Super + W`

You are not trying to do it fast.

You are trying to make it familiar.

That is enough.

## You Do Not Need The Whole Keyboard Map

The hotkey sheet is still there.

The manual is still there.

That means you do not need to carry the whole system in your head.

You just need a few movement patterns that make the machine feel like a place
you can operate without panic.

That is the threshold we are crossing here.

Not perfection.

Usability.

That is a much better target.

## Keep Moving, Then Go Deeper

If you can:

- change workspaces
- close a window
- fullscreen something
- copy and paste reliably
- retrieve clipboard history

you are already in a different relationship with the machine than you were in
last chapter.

That is progress.

And it matters because the next chapter brings in the terminal more directly.

It is much easier to meet the terminal when the rest of the system no longer
feels slippery.
