# Research Support Memo

> Curated source-backed support for *The Business Growth Playbook*.
>
> Purpose:
> use research as reinforcement, not as the driver of the manuscript.
> This file is for exact, citable support behind key doctrines and chapter
> claims. It is not a dumping ground for interesting AI articles.

---

## Usage Rules

- Prefer primary sources.
- Separate what the sources directly show from what the book is inferring.
- Use studies for spice and authority, not to replace the book's lived proof.
- Do not cite any statistic or finding in prose unless it is logged in
  `reference-ledger.md`.
- When the manuscript goes beyond the source, label that as an inference.

## Theme 1 — AI Often Works Best as Augmentation, Not Only Automation

### Claim

AI can materially raise productivity and quality in knowledge work, and the
biggest gains often come through augmentation or expertise diffusion rather than
pure headcount substitution.

### Evidence That Supports It

- Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond, *Generative AI at Work* (NBER Working Paper
  31161, 2023) studied 5,179 customer support agents and found an average 14%
  productivity increase, with a 34% improvement for novice and lower-skilled
  workers and minimal impact for the most experienced workers. The paper also
  reports suggestive evidence that the AI system disseminated the best
  practices of more able workers and may have helped worker learning.
- Dell'Acqua et al., *Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier* (HBS Working
  Paper 24-013) found that consultants using GPT-4 on tasks inside the model's
  capability frontier were more productive, faster, and produced higher-quality
  outputs. The strongest gains were often for lower performers, but top-half
  performers also improved.
- Cui et al., *The Effects of Generative AI on High-Skilled Work: Evidence from
  Three Field Experiments with Software Developers* (working paper draft, 2026)
  report a 26.08% increase in completed tasks across three randomized field
  experiments involving 4,867 developers, with stronger gains for less
  experienced developers.
- Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson, *Building Pro-Worker Artificial Intelligence*
  (NBER Working Paper 34854, 2026) argues analytically that AI's potential to
  extend human judgment, enable new tasks, and accelerate skill acquisition is
  substantial and currently underexploited.

### Evidence That Complicates It

- The HBS "jagged technological frontier" paper also shows that performance can
  worsen when people use AI on tasks outside the model's real capability
  frontier.
- The customer-support and developer studies do not prove that every enterprise
  should preserve every role. They show heterogeneous gains, not a universal
  anti-reduction rule.
- The Acemoglu/Autor/Johnson paper is analytical and normative, not a field
  experiment.

### Confidence

Strong.

### Research Gap

The strongest direct evidence is still task- and role-specific. The book can
 credibly say augmentation is often underused, but should avoid claiming that
 augmentation always dominates substitution in every setting.

## Theme 2 — Lower Friction Can Raise the Relative Value of Judgment and Context

### Claim

When AI compresses repeatable effort, the relative value of judgment, context,
and trusted human oversight can rise rather than fall.

### Evidence That Supports It

- Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond's customer-support study is consistent with
  this pattern. The biggest gains went to less experienced workers, and the
  paper suggests the tool spread the best practices of stronger workers.
- Dell'Acqua et al. show that high-human-capital consultants improved
  materially on tasks inside the model frontier, indicating that AI can amplify
  already capable workers rather than merely replace them.
- Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson explicitly distinguish between automating,
  expertise-leveling, labor-augmenting, and new-task-creating technologies.
  That framework supports the book's distinction between compression and
  multiplication.

### Evidence That Complicates It

- The empirical studies above show productivity and quality effects, not the
  whole organizational downstream value of judgment, trust, or continuity.
- The book's stronger claim about "range" remains partly an inference drawn
  from lived experience plus the studies' measured gains.

### Confidence

Moderate to strong.

### Research Gap

There is still less direct field evidence on amplified judgment, cross-domain
 translation, and internal teaching than on straightforward productivity gains.
 Those parts of the manuscript should rely more heavily on lived examples.

## Theme 3 — Tacit Knowledge and Organizational Memory Matter Economically

### Claim

Some of the most valuable organizational knowledge is tacit, hard to codify,
and economically important. Losing it can weaken performance even when the loss
does not show up clearly at first.

### Evidence That Supports It

- Zucker, Darby, and Armstrong, *Commercializing Knowledge: University Science,
  Knowledge Capture, and Firm Performance in Biotechnology* (NBER Working Paper
  8499; published in *Management Science*, 2002) found that tacit knowledge
  capture was a strong predictor of firm success in biotechnology. Their
  evidence supports the claim that complex, high-value knowledge does not move
  cleanly through documents alone.
- Agrawal and Muthulingam, *Does Organizational Forgetting Affect Vendor
  Quality Performance?* (*Manufacturing & Service Operations Management*, 2015)
  found that quality gains depreciated over time and that forgetting was lower
  when knowledge was embedded in technology than when it was embedded in
  routines or organizational members. This supports the book's warning that
  organizations can quietly lose valuable know-how.
- Brynjolfsson and Hitzig, *AI's Use of Knowledge in Society* (chapter in *The
  Economics of Transformative AI*, 2025) argues that AI can codify knowledge
  that was previously tacit, but the very framing implies that the locus and
  ownership of such knowledge matters.

### Evidence That Complicates It

- Tacit knowledge is not always impossible to capture. Some of it can be
  codified, formalized, or transferred with the right systems.
- The older tacit-knowledge literature is not specific to generative AI. The
  book must not overstate what those sources prove about today's tools.

### Confidence

Moderate.

### Research Gap

The book's `Knowledge Carriers` concept is more concrete and people-shaped than
 the literature's vocabulary. That is fine, but it means the exact term is the
 book's own contribution rather than a standard research construct.

## Theme 4 — Incentives Can Bias Firms Toward Automation-First AI

### Claim

Firms may underinvest in worker-amplifying AI because incentives, ideology, and
measurement systems make automation-first strategies easier to justify.

### Evidence That Supports It

- Acemoglu, Autor, and Johnson explicitly argue that misaligned firm and
  developer incentives, path dependence, and a pro-automation ideology can lead
  to underinvestment in pro-worker AI.
- Bick, Blandin, and Deming, *The Rapid Adoption of Generative AI* (NBER
  Working Paper 32966, 2024/2025) shows that workplace adoption is already
  widespread and fast-moving, which increases the practical importance of the
  incentive question because leaders are making these allocation choices now,
  not in a hypothetical future.

### Evidence That Complicates It

- The "pro-automation ideology" claim is an analytical argument, not a field
  experiment.
- Rapid adoption does not by itself prove a savings-first bias. It shows the
  urgency of the managerial decision surface.

### Confidence

Moderate.

### Research Gap

The book's specific claim that boards and CFOs reward visible savings faster
 than capability is mostly a practical observation from lived enterprise
 experience, not a claim we currently have a clean causal study for.

## Theme 5 — Fast Adoption Does Not Resolve the Strategic Question

### Claim

Generative AI is being adopted quickly, but adoption alone does not answer what
leaders should do with the capacity it creates.

### Evidence That Supports It

- Bick, Blandin, and Deming find fast adoption in the U.S. population and at
  work, with time savings equivalent to roughly 1.4% of total work hours in
  their survey window. That supports the book's argument that new capacity is
  already showing up.
- The customer-support, consultant, and software-developer studies all show
  measurable gains, but none of them say those gains must be harvested as labor
  reduction. That gap is exactly where this book's argument begins.

### Evidence That Complicates It

- The measured gains in these studies do not automatically convert into
  enterprise-wide abundance. Complementary management decisions still matter.

### Confidence

Strong on rapid adoption and localized gains; moderate on broader strategic
 implications.

### Research Gap

This is where the book is contributing a managerial doctrine:
 `capacity allocation` is the missing leadership decision, not a direct
 research conclusion from any single paper.

- Introduction
- Chapter 1
- Chapter 6
- Conclusion

## Claims To Avoid Unless Better Sourced

- "AI always creates more jobs than it destroys."
- "Most firms are using AI mainly for layoffs."
- "Tacit knowledge cannot be captured."
- "Human amplification is always more profitable than automation."
- any precise macro forecast about abundance, employment, or GDP unless the
  exact model and source are clearly introduced.
