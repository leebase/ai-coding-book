# Audience Review

> Initial audience review for *The Business Growth Playbook* concept package.

---

## Lens 1 — Transformation / Operations Leader

### What Lands

- The pressure to prove AI through immediate savings feels real.
- `What do we do with the capacity AI creates?` is a useful operating question.
- `Efficiency Extractor` versus `Capacity Investor` gives this reader a clear
  leadership mirror.
- The Lewis and Clark pattern feels practical because it offers a bounded first
  move instead of demanding enterprise-wide transformation theater.

### What This Reader Will Push Back On

- They will want clearer guidance on how to defend capacity investment when the
  CFO wants savings first.
- They will need examples of what capacity reinvestment looks like in actual
  budget, staffing, and program decisions.
- They may resist anything that sounds morally right but operationally vague.

### Review Outcome

Strong fit for the primary audience if the book keeps converting doctrine into
Monday-morning leadership choices.

---

## Lens 2 — Business Unit Leader / GM

### What Lands

- The idea that visible savings can hide invisible loss feels true.
- `Don't Eat Your Seed Corn` is memorable and easy to translate into line-level
  decisions.
- The promise of amplified people and small trusted teams is attractive because
  it sounds like growth, not just program management.

### What This Reader Will Push Back On

- They will want clearer linkage to revenue, customers, and market-facing
  growth.
- They may find transformation language too central unless it is connected to
  business outcomes quickly.
- They will want to know how to identify the few people worth amplifying first.

### Review Outcome

Good fit if the book keeps the growth promise visible and does not get trapped
inside enterprise-program jargon.

---

## Lens 3 — CFO / Cost-Focused Executive

### What Lands

- The book acknowledges that savings pressure is rational rather than foolish.
- `capacity` is a better operating variable than vague innovation talk.
- `capacity misallocation` can create real tension because it reframes some
  successful-looking savings as strategic underinvestment.

### What This Reader Will Push Back On

- They will suspect the book is too soft unless it explains why capability
  investment should earn economic respect.
- They will ask how to measure success when the benefit is not immediate
  headcount reduction.
- They may hear `human abundance` as aspiration unless it is grounded in
  consequences and examples.

### Review Outcome

This is the pressure-test audience. If this reader feels comfortable too
quickly, the book is not sharp enough yet.

---

## Lens 4 — Consultant Partner Supporting the Enterprise

### What Lands

- The paired-book framing gives this audience a clear place without making them
  the main subject.
- The book's doctrine aligns with the best version of what a consulting partner
  should help the client do.
- The Lewis and Clark pattern gives partners a credible way to propose bounded,
  empowerment-oriented work.

### What This Reader Will Push Back On

- They will want more explicit cues about how enterprise leaders should judge
  whether a partner is helping invest capacity or merely harvest savings.
- They may try to pull the book back toward seller logic if the manuscript is
  not careful.

### Review Outcome

Useful secondary audience, but the book should never let this lens dominate the
center of gravity.

---

## Cross-Audience Readout

### What Survives All Lenses

- AI-created capacity is the real decision surface
- visible savings can hide invisible failure
- `Don't Eat Your Seed Corn`
- amplified people matter more than reduction rhetoric admits
- the future organization may be built from smaller, higher-trust,
  disproportionately capable teams

### Residual Risks

- not enough concrete measures for capability investment
- too much inspiration and not enough operating detail
- drifting back toward consulting-centered framing

### Test for the Manuscript

The concept is working when:

- a transformation leader sees a usable playbook
- a GM sees a path to growth
- a CFO feels tension
- a consultant can recognize the doctrine without becoming the book's subject
