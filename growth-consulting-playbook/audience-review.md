# Audience Review

> AuthorFlow audience review for the canonical concept.

---

## Big Consulting Review

### What Lands

- The argument that delivery economics are under pressure feels credible.
- The distinction between commodity delivery and higher-value judgment work fits
  large-firm realities.
- The emphasis on pricing, operating model, and client conversation makes the
  book feel serious rather than rhetorical.

### Likely Pushback

- Large firms will say relationships, brand, and integrated scale still create
  a moat beyond delivery compression.
- Some will argue they are already repositioning, so the book must avoid
  sounding like it discovered the problem late.
- They may resist any framing that sounds anti-automation rather than
  anti-reductionism.

### What Needs To Be Explicit

- The book is not arguing that all delivery work disappears.
- It is arguing that delivery alone is no longer enough as a business model.
- The large-firm story should distinguish between elite advisory, large-scale
  execution, and labor-arbitrage implementation.

---

## Mid-Market Consulting Review

### What Lands

- This is the strongest natural audience.
- The threat feels urgent because mid-market firms are more exposed to delivery
  revenue and have less room to fund long transitions.
- The book's practical promise feels most valuable here: what do we sell when
  the old work gets cheaper?

### Likely Pushback

- Mid-market readers will ask whether the repositioning advice assumes more
  capital, brand, or talent depth than they actually have.
- They will want examples that feel achievable without giant internal AI
  investments.
- They will resist language that sounds like analyst theory disconnected from
  firm survival.

### What Needs To Be Explicit

- The book's default reader should likely be a boutique-to-mid-market consulting
  leader.
- The playbook must emphasize sequence, tradeoffs, and what can be done with
  limited resources.
- The book should show how a smaller firm can reposition before it can
  industrialize.

---

## Individual Consultant Review

### What Lands

- The labor-to-leverage shift is highly relevant.
- The ideas about human amplification, hidden capacity, and moving beyond
  delivery-for-hire are motivating.
- The book's voice feels strongest when it sounds like a reflective
  practitioner rather than a firm.

### Likely Pushback

- Solo operators will reject guidance that assumes formal service lines, change
  programs, or partner committees.
- They will ask: what do I do if I am the brand, the seller, and the delivery
  engine all at once?
- If the book gets too institutional, they will stop seeing themselves in it.

### What Needs To Be Explicit

- The ideas must scale down, not just up.
- The book should include a path for independents: reposition your offer,
  productize judgment, and stop selling undifferentiated labor.
- The tone should remain personal enough that an individual consultant feels
  invited in.

---

## Cross-Audience Synthesis

### What Survives All Three Lenses

- The Efficiency Trap
- knowledge and continuity as strategic assets
- delivery compression as a real business-model issue
- the need to redirect productivity rather than only reduce cost
- the move from labor sale to judgment and capability sale

### Main Risk

The book could drift into one of two failures:

1. Too Big Consulting:
   - sophisticated but unusable for most firms
2. Too solo-practitioner:
   - vivid but not serious enough for firm leaders

### Recommendation

Frame the **primary audience** explicitly as:

**boutique-to-mid-market consulting leaders**

Then write the book so:

- Big Consulting sees the strategic seriousness
- independent consultants can still translate the core moves into practice

This keeps the book sharp instead of diluted.
