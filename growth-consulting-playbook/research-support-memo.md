# Research Support Memo

> Integrated research synthesis from the Claude, Gemini, and ChatGPT research memos.

---

## Overall Assessment

The book thesis is strong and differentiated.

What the research supports most clearly is not every tactical claim, but the
larger pattern:

- AI is putting pressure on delivery-heavy consulting economics
- automation-first thinking is tempting and widespread
- augmentation often creates stronger long-term outcomes than naive replacement
- knowledge loss is a real strategic risk
- the consulting business model is being forced upward toward judgment,
  orchestration, and outcomes

The strongest differentiator remains the **reflexive move**:
apply these ideas to consulting firms themselves, not just to their clients.

The ChatGPT research memo sharpened two important aspects of the argument:

- it connected the disruption back to classic consulting leverage economics
- it clarified that the strongest evidence is about pressure on **routine,
  bounded, repeatable delivery work**, not the disappearance of all software or
  all consulting work

---

## Strongest Claims

These claims appear strong enough to feature prominently once exact citations
are verified.

### 1. Delivery Compression Is Real

- AI is reducing the cost and increasing the speed of technical production.
- Even where productivity gains are uneven, the market is repricing delivery
  labor.
- This puts direct pressure on billing-hour and implementation-heavy models.
- The older professional-services literature matters here too: if AI compresses
  the value of junior and repeatable delivery work, it destabilizes the classic
  leverage model of the consulting pyramid.

### 2. Automation-First Is an Incomplete Strategy

- Multiple sources support the distinction between automation and augmentation.
- Acemoglu, Brynjolfsson, Harvard/BCG, and MIT-adjacent work all point toward a
  more human-complementary path creating more durable value.

### 3. Knowledge Loss Matters

- The tacit knowledge literature is mature and highly relevant.
- Organizational memory, relationships, and undocumented judgment are strategic
  assets.
- The connection to AI-led cuts in consulting is underdeveloped in published
  literature, which creates white space for the book.

### 4. Consulting's Model Is Already Shifting

- Major firms are reorganizing, investing in AI, and changing service models.
- Industry analysts are explicitly describing a shift away from pure
  labor-delivery economics.
- The best-supported version of the market thesis is **value migration**, not
  consulting extinction.

---

## High-Value Evidence To Use Carefully

These are powerful, but should be verified against the original sources before
they become chapter-level headline claims.

- McKinsey: 20-30% contraction in core tech services
- McKinsey: $100B-$400B new value pools
- METR: experienced developers slower with AI while perceiving themselves
  faster
- Harvard/BCG: consultants with AI completing more tasks faster and at higher
  quality
- reported regret / rehiring / knowledge-reconstruction claims tied to AI-led
  layoffs

These are useful, but they need citation hygiene because some supporting paths
run through summaries, commentary, or secondary writeups.

---

## Claims That Need Caution

### 1. "Growth Beats Cuts" in Consulting Specifically

This is directionally well supported, but not yet proven with consulting-firm
specific evidence at the level the book may eventually want.

### 2. "Mid-Market Firms Are the Most Exposed"

This is plausible and strategically useful, but still needs more direct support
or interview evidence.

### 3. "The Most Valuable Employees After AI Are X"

This is partly supported through augmentation and human-capability research, but
the consulting-specific role implications should be stated carefully.

### 4. "Software Delivery No Longer Pays the Bills"

This is a strong frame and a useful subtitle, but the supporting evidence is
best for routine and repeatable delivery work rather than every form of complex
software or consulting execution. The manuscript should keep the line sharp
without turning it into an unsupported absolute.

---

## Best Use of the Research

The research should do four things for the book:

1. prove the shift is real
2. prove the reduction-only response is incomplete
3. prove knowledge and continuity are not soft concerns
4. prove the opportunity is larger than efficiency alone

The book should not pretend the research already proves every tactical part of
the playbook.

- examples of pricing and service-portfolio redesign below Big Consulting scale
