# Product Definition: The Growth Consulting Playbook

> **What we're building and why.**

---

## The Book in One Sentence

A thesis-driven business book for consulting leaders that argues AI will compress the value of software delivery work, and that firms will win not by helping clients cut deeper, but by helping them turn AI-enabled productivity into growth.

---

## The Problem

Consulting firms have spent decades monetizing labor: coding hours, implementation work, system delivery, and the human effort wrapped around transformation.

AI changes the economics of that model. As software delivery and workflow automation become faster, cheaper, and more abundant, the old equation weakens. Clients see the first visible win immediately: reduce labor cost. Consultants can sell that win easily because it is measurable, familiar, and politically attractive.

But that logic contains a trap.

When companies treat AI productivity mainly as a reason to cut headcount, they do not only remove cost. They often remove institutional knowledge, continuity, judgment, and future growth capacity. Consulting firms that help clients optimize for this short-term efficiency can also shrink the very client capability base that would have sustained future higher-value consulting work.

The result is a strategic mismatch:

- firms sell reduction when they should be designing growth
- clients buy efficiency when they need capability expansion
- AI is framed as substitution when its deeper value may be amplification

---

## The Thesis

> The future of consulting belongs to firms that help clients turn AI productivity into growth, not just labor reduction.

More sharply:

> When software delivery no longer pays the bills, consulting value moves upstream from labor delivery to judgment, capability building, workflow redesign, knowledge retention, and growth architecture.

This book argues that:

- the traditional delivery-heavy consulting model is under structural pressure
- the most tempting AI move is often the wrong strategic move
- firms that help clients cut too deeply risk "eating the seed corn"
- AI changes which employees are most important to retain
- the strongest long-term consulting model is built around empowerment, multiplication, and reinvestment

---

## The Reader

**Primary reader:**
- Consulting firm founders
- Managing partners
- Practice leaders
- Digital transformation leaders inside consulting firms
- Boutique and mid-market consulting leaders trying to reposition before delivery work commoditizes

**Secondary reader:**
- Enterprise executives buying AI and transformation services
- Internal strategy, operations, or transformation leaders
- Professionals designing AI enablement programs inside large organizations

**What they believe when they open the book:**
- AI is reshaping consulting, but the path forward is unclear
- cost reduction is the most obvious and defensible AI use case
- software delivery will remain valuable, but perhaps not enough on its own
- their firm needs a strategy, not just a tool policy

**What they can do when they close it:**
- recognize the efficiency trap before building services around it
- reframe AI engagements around growth, knowledge, and capability
- identify the kinds of employees and client capabilities that become more valuable after AI
- redesign parts of a consulting portfolio away from commodity delivery
- speak credibly to clients about empowerment, reinvestment, and long-term value

---

## Why Now

- AI capability has advanced far enough that delivery compression is no longer hypothetical
- consulting leaders are being pushed to explain what their firms will sell when coding and implementation work become less scarce
- many organizations are still treating AI primarily as a labor-reduction tool
- the market is early enough that a sharp reframing can still shape strategy rather than merely comment on history

This is a now-book, not a future-book.

---

## What Makes This Book Different

Most AI business books fall into one of these categories:

- trend commentary about disruption
- enterprise AI adoption advice
- broad management optimism or anxiety
- technical books about tools and workflows

This book is different in three ways:

1. It turns the AI productivity argument back onto the consulting business model itself.
2. It connects efficiency, knowledge loss, continuity, workforce design, and consulting economics into one system.
3. It argues from both strategic logic and lived practitioner experience, not just market commentary.

The likely differentiator is not the claim that AI changes work. It is the claim that consulting firms can accidentally help clients destroy future growth capacity and commoditize their own relevance if they optimize for reduction alone.

---

## Book Promise

This book will give consulting leaders:

- language for the trap their firms and clients are walking into
- a better mental model for how AI changes value creation
- named concepts and frameworks they can use in strategy and client conversations
- practical guidance for repositioning service lines, engagements, and talent
- a way to think about AI as human amplification, not just automation

This is not a prompt book or an AI tools manual.

It is a repositioning book.

## Likely Structural Shape

The working shape is:

### Part I — The Trap

- Why the delivery economy is weakening
- Why efficiency is the first tempting move
- What gets lost when firms cut too deep

### Part II — The Reframe

- labor to leverage
- judgment versus code
- substitution versus empowerment
- hidden or underutilized human capability

### Part III — The Playbook

- redesigning service portfolios
- reframing engagements
- protecting knowledge and continuity
- building growth-oriented AI offerings

### Part IV — Firm Transformation / Strategic Future

- pricing and revenue model risk
- talent implications
- client conversation shifts
- what winning firms will look like

This structure is still provisional and will be refined as research and story selection continue.

## Evidence Strategy

This book should be built from a mix of:

- citable thought leadership and research
- consulting-industry analysis
- firsthand stories from Lee's own work and AI practice
- eventual interviews, especially with mid-market consulting leaders

Current working assumption:
- there is already enough external evidence to support the broad thesis
- the strongest gap is likely mid-market consulting evidence and practical operating detail

---

## Constraints

- The book must stay sharper than generic AI commentary
- It should not become a consulting memoir
- It should not depend on one firm's model, one vendor, or one platform
- It must balance strategic argument with practical operating consequences
- Claims should be as strong as the evidence allows, and explicitly provisional where evidence is still thin

---

## Success Criteria

The book succeeds if a reader finishes it able to:

1. Explain why AI-driven efficiency can become a strategic trap
2. See why cutting employees also cuts knowledge, continuity, and future leverage
3. Recognize which capabilities and people become more valuable after AI
4. Reframe consulting value away from delivery hours and toward multiplication
5. Identify concrete changes they should make to offerings, engagement design, and talent strategy

The project succeeds if, in addition:

1. the book has a clear and differentiated market position
2. the ideas are strong enough to support consulting offerings and thought leadership beyond the manuscript
3. the method used to build the book is captured well enough to later describe as `AuthorFlow`

---

## Out of Scope

- A broad survey of every AI tool vendor
- Deep technical tutorials on model building or coding assistants
- A full academic treatment of labor economics
- A generalized future-of-work book for all industries
- A pure memoir about Lee's AI journey

Those may inform the book, but they are not the product.

---

## Open Questions

These should remain explicit until more research lands:

1. How narrowly should the primary audience be defined: consulting firms only, or consulting plus enterprise buyers?
2. How much of the book should focus on mid-market firms versus large consultancies?
3. Which candidate framework should become the signature model?
4. How personal should Lee's lived examples become in the final manuscript?
5. Which claims need firsthand interviews before they should be stated confidently?
