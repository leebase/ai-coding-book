# Research Memo: Thought Leadership on AI, Consulting, and Growth

**Prepared for:** Lee Harrington — *The Growth Consulting Playbook*
**Date:** March 27, 2026
**Scope:** Deep research synthesis across economics, consulting industry analysis, organizational behavior, and AI productivity literature

---

## 1. Executive Summary

- **The core thesis is well-supported but unevenly distributed across existing literature.** The individual pillars — AI compressing software delivery costs, the danger of headcount-reduction strategies, the superiority of augmentation over automation — each have strong backing. What is genuinely novel is the synthesis: applying these ideas specifically to the consulting firm's own business model transformation, not just to their clients'.

- **Daron Acemoglu and Simon Johnson's "Power and Progress" (2023) is the single most important adjacent work.** Their argument that AI is being used "too much for automation and not enough for providing expertise and information to workers" directly undergirds the book's thesis. Acemoglu's concept of "so-so technology" — automation that saves money but doesn't meaningfully increase productivity — is a foundational frame.

- **Erik Brynjolfsson's "productivity J-curve" provides the temporal argument.** His work at the Stanford Digital Economy Lab demonstrates that transformative technologies suppress measured productivity during an investment phase before entering a harvest phase. His 2026 Financial Times analysis estimates US productivity growth hit roughly 2.7% in 2025, nearly double the prior decade's average, suggesting AI's harvest phase may be arriving.

- **McKinsey's own research projects a 20-30% contraction in core tech services** due to agentic AI, while simultaneously identifying $100-400 billion in new value pools from business function transformation with human-agent operating models. This is the most concrete industry evidence for the "grow, don't shrink" thesis.

- **The Harvard/BCG "Jagged Frontier" study (Dell'Acqua et al., 2023) provides the empirical anchor for augmentation.** Consultants using AI completed 12.2% more tasks, 25.1% faster, with 40% higher quality — but only when AI was applied to appropriate tasks. Below-average performers saw a 43% improvement. This is powerful evidence that AI is a capability multiplier, not a headcount reducer.

- **HFS Research's "Services-as-Software" (SaS) framework, coined by Phil Fersht, names the structural shift.** SaS is projected to become a $1.5 trillion market by 2035, creating existential risk for "hours factories" but the biggest growth opportunity in a generation for firms that productize expertise.

- **Nitin Seth's Fast Company analysis of the McKinsey layoffs (December 2025) provides the consulting-specific alarm bell.** Seth, a former McKinsey Director, argues the industry's center of gravity is shifting from strategy (where information scarcity was the moat) to technology-enabled execution.

- **The tacit knowledge literature is deep and well-established but under-applied to the AI era.** Research consistently shows that layoffs destroy institutional memory, with organizations losing not just skills but internal and external relationship networks. The connection between AI-driven layoffs and knowledge destruction is an under-explored intersection.

- **A significant counterargument exists:** the METR study (Becker et al., 2025) found experienced open-source developers were 19% *slower* with AI tools, while *perceiving* they were 20% faster. This challenges simplistic narratives about AI productivity but supports the argument that implementation quality — not just adoption — determines outcomes.

- **The book's unique positioning opportunity:** No existing work combines the consulting firm's own business model vulnerability with a prescriptive growth framework. Most literature either addresses client-side transformation or macro-economic effects. The gap is practitioner-level guidance for consulting firm leaders navigating their own disruption.

- **The "Services-as-Software" convergence and the Acemoglu/Brynjolfsson augmentation debate create a strong intellectual spine** for a book that argues: the firms that help clients use AI to grow (not just cut) will themselves need to practice what they preach.

## 2. Core Thesis Framing

### Proposed Thesis (Refined)

AI is driving the marginal cost of software delivery toward zero. Consulting firms that monetize coding labor and implementation hours face structural margin compression. The instinctive response — using AI productivity gains to reduce headcount — destroys institutional knowledge, client continuity, and long-term growth capacity. The stronger strategy is to redirect AI-driven productivity into growth: empowering employees, redesigning work, and creating new value. Consulting firms that master this internally and help clients do the same will become more valuable, not less.

### Strongest Adjacent Ideas Already in Market

1. **Acemoglu & Johnson, "Power and Progress" (2023):** Technology creates growth, but who captures it depends on whether AI is used for automation (replacing people) or augmentation (empowering people). The "wrong direction" argument.

2. **Brynjolfsson's J-Curve Hypothesis:** Transformative technologies suppress productivity during investment phases, then produce exponential returns during harvest phases. Companies must invest in process redesign and training before seeing returns.

3. **HFS Research / Phil Fersht, "Services-as-Software":** The blurring of people-based services with technology-delivered outcomes, projected as a $1.5T market by 2035.

4. **McKinsey, "Reimagining the Value Proposition of Tech Services" (December 2025):** The 20-30% contraction thesis for core tech services alongside the $100-400B new value pool in business function transformation.

5. **Dell'Acqua et al., "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier" (2023):** The empirical case for AI augmentation of knowledge workers, with the critical caveat that AI applied outside its capability frontier actively harms performance.

### What Appears Original About This Angle

- **The reflexive application:** Most writing tells consulting firms how to help their clients with AI. This book tells consulting firms how to save themselves — and argues the two are inseparable.
- **The growth-not-cuts prescription as an operational framework**, not just a philosophical position. Acemoglu says "use AI to augment." This book would say "here is how, specifically, for a mid-market consulting firm."
- **The explicit connection between knowledge loss literature and AI-driven layoffs** in consulting. The organizational behavior research on tacit knowledge erosion is deep but has not been applied to this specific moment.
- **The "Service as Software" framing applied to the consulting firm's own operations**, not just to their clients' technology stacks.

## 3. Key Thinkers and Institutions

### Daron Acemoglu (MIT, Nobel Laureate 2024)
**Why he matters:** The most credible academic voice on AI's labor market effects. Co-developed the task-based framework that is now the standard analytical lens for automation vs. augmentation economics.
**Relevant argument:** AI is being directed toward "so-so automation" that modestly improves productivity while displacing workers, rather than toward creating new productive tasks. The productivity gains from this path will be modest (0.07% annual TFP growth over 10 years). The better path — "machine usefulness" — would complement workers rather than replace them.
**Relationship to thesis:** Strongly supports. His framework provides the economic theory underlying the "growth not cuts" argument.
**Key sources:**
- Acemoglu, D. "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI." *Economic Policy*, January 2025. ([MIT](https://economics.mit.edu/sites/default/files/2024-04/The%20Simple%20Macroeconomics%20of%20AI.pdf))

But most businesses still use AI for narrow tasks; a small cohort of "power users" compress weeks of work into hours. The gap between early adopters and laggards will widen.
**Relationship to thesis:** Supports with nuance. Provides the optimistic case that firms redirecting productivity into growth (not cuts) will capture outsized value.

## 4. Evidence by Theme

### 4.1 AI Reduces the Cost of Software Delivery

The evidence here is strong but more nuanced than the narrative suggests.

**Productivity gains are real but variable.** GitHub data (November 2025–February 2026) shows AI-authored code now comprises 26.9% of all production code, up from 22% the prior quarter. Daily AI users see roughly a third of their merged code written by AI. Developer onboarding time (measured by time to 10th pull request) has been cut in half since Q1 2024.

**The counterevidence is important.** The METR randomized controlled trial (Becker et al., 2025) found experienced open-source developers were 19% slower when using AI, while perceiving themselves 20% faster. METR's February 2026 update acknowledges severe selection effects in their follow-up study — developers increasingly refuse to work without AI, making the control condition harder to study. The 2025 Stack Overflow survey shows positive sentiment toward AI tools dropped to 60% from 70%+, with nearly half of developers distrusting AI output accuracy.

**Cost compression in delivery is structural even if individual productivity gains are debatable.** The argument does not require that every developer becomes more productive. It requires that the market price of software delivery falls as AI-assisted delivery becomes the norm. Salesforce CEO Marc Benioff's late-2024 statement that the company would pause hiring software engineers due to AI efficiency gains is a leading indicator of this market dynamic. Brynjolfsson et al. (2025) found roughly 15-16% employment declines for early-career workers in AI-exposed occupations.

**Key takeaway for the book:** The cost of software delivery is falling not because every developer is more productive, but because the market is repricing delivery labor. This makes the "don't just cut heads" argument more urgent, not less.

### 4.2 AI and Headcount Reduction

The instinct to use AI for headcount reduction is well-documented.

McKinsey's 2025 State of AI survey found a median of 17% of respondents reporting workforce decreases in the past year due to AI, with 30% expecting decreases in the coming year. The Big Four consulting firms themselves have reduced entry-level hiring significantly: KPMG by 29%, Deloitte by 18%, EY by 11%. When McKinsey launched Lilli in 2023, it cut over 5,000 jobs the same year. Accenture laid off 11,000 employees while simultaneously hiring 77,000 in AI/data roles.

Brynjolfsson's "Canaries in the Coal Mine" research (2025) provides the most granular evidence: early-career workers (ages 22-25) in AI-exposed occupations show large relative employment declines of 15-16%, even controlling for firm-level shocks. The adjustment shows up more in employment than wages, concentrated in occupations where AI use appears more automating than augmenting.

**The Deloitte Australia scandal (October 2025) illustrates the billing model tension.** Deloitte charged the Australian government AUD $440,000 for a report largely produced using AI, sparking parliamentary inquiry. This is a microcosm of the broader business model tension: when AI does the work, how do you justify the bill?

### 4.3 Augmentation vs. Replacement

This is the richest vein of evidence supporting the book's thesis.

**The Harvard/BCG "Jagged Frontier" study remains the gold standard.** 758 BCG consultants using GPT-4 completed 12.2% more tasks, 25.1% faster, with 40% higher quality on tasks within the AI frontier. Below-average performers gained 43%; above-average gained 17%. This is the strongest evidence for AI as a skill leveler and productivity multiplier.

**MIT Sloan's EPOCH framework (March 2025)** identifies five categories of uniquely human capabilities (Empathy, Perception, Originality, Connectedness, Hope/Vision/Leadership) and demonstrates that tasks requiring these capabilities are increasing, not decreasing, in the economy.

**Harvard's Srinivasan et al. (2024/2025)** analyzed US job postings from 2019 through March 2025 and found that AI-related skills are increasing in augmentation-prone roles, while demand for structured, repetitive tasks is falling. The paper's explicit recommendation: companies should treat AI as an augmentation tool, not merely a cost-cutting measure.

**Brynjolfsson et al. (2025), "Generative AI at Work,"** studied AI deployment in a customer service context and found that AI assistants increased worker productivity by 14%, with the largest gains for novice and low-skill workers. The tool made recommendations to agents, who then interacted with customers — a textbook augmentation pattern.

**Anthropic's own data (analyzed by the Washington Post, July 2025)** showed that across 1 million conversations at the end of 2024, AI was augmenting or automating roughly 25% of tasks across all jobs. Augmentation use remained significantly higher than automation use for almost all occupations.

**Acemoglu and Johnson's central argument** remains that the choice between automation and augmentation is not technologically determined — it is a business decision shaped by incentives, tax policy, and organizational culture. Their warning: automation appeals to managers seeking consistency and reduced labor costs, but augmentation produces greater long-term productivity and shared prosperity.

### 4.4 Knowledge Loss, Continuity, and Tacit Knowledge

The organizational behavior literature on knowledge loss is mature but has not been systematically connected to AI-driven layoffs.

**Foundational concepts:** Michael Polanyi's concept of tacit knowledge (1966) — knowledge that is difficult to formalize, communicate, or codify — remains the theoretical cornerstone. Researchers have described organizational knowledge loss through metaphors including "organizational Alzheimer's," "enterprise dementia" (Girard, 2005), and "corporate amnesia" (Leon, 2020).

**Empirical evidence on turnover and knowledge loss:** A comprehensive literature review in *The Learning Organization* journal (2023) synthesizes decades of empirical research showing that departures of critical employees — those possessing tacit, experiential knowledge and key relationship networks — produce the most damaging effects. The damage extends to client dissatisfaction, design faults in new products, and loss of future business opportunities. Knowledge loss is particularly difficult to manage because organizations cannot predict which employees will leave or when (Leon et al., 2017), and departing employees are often unwilling to share their knowledge.

**Quantified impact:** A Sinequa survey of 1,000 IT managers found 67% were concerned about knowledge and expertise loss when people leave, and 71% agreed the Great Resignation was contributing to organizational knowledge loss. Gallup research cited by SHRMLabs estimates the cost of replacing one employee ranges from one-half to two times their annual salary. Organizations with 30,000 employees can expect to lose $72 million annually due to knowledge-loss-driven inefficiencies.

**The specific connection to AI layoffs:** This is an underexplored area. One emerging thread is the work on Human-AI Collaboration (HAIC) as a mechanism for preserving expert knowledge when subject matter experts depart. The argument is that AI can serve as a training mechanism for transferring tacit, specialized knowledge to novices. But this requires the experts to still be present during the transition — cutting them first destroys the possibility.

### 4.5 Productivity Redirected into Growth

**Brynjolfsson provides the macro frame.** His J-curve hypothesis argues that companies in the "harvest phase" — those that have invested in process redesign, worker training, and organizational restructuring — will see exponential returns. The key insight for the book: the choice of *what to do with productivity gains* determines whether a firm enters the harvest phase or simply captures a one-time cost reduction.

**BCG's research of 1,250 companies (2024)** found that only 5% are achieving AI value at scale, but these "future-built" organizations generate 1.7x more revenue growth than competitors. They are 12 times more likely to have deeply engaged C-level executives and 3 times more likely to have appointed a chief AI officer.

**Accenture's research** shows companies leveraging generative AI and automation achieve 2.5x higher revenue growth and 2.4x greater productivity than peers.

**The Penn Wharton Budget Model (September 2025)** projects AI will increase productivity and GDP by 1.5% by 2035, with sectors more exposed to AI (including professional services) growing faster than the rest of the economy. This provides macro support for the idea that AI-enabled professional services firms can capture growth rather than just cost savings.

**The "growth from productivity" vs. "cost savings from productivity" debate** maps directly onto Acemoglu's automation vs. augmentation framework. The firms that treat AI as a way to do more with the same people (growth) rather than the same with fewer people (savings) are positioned to capture the larger share of value.

### 4.6 Future of Consulting and Professional Services

**McKinsey's December 2025 analysis** is the most detailed industry projection: 20-30% contraction in core tech services, offset by $100-400B in new value pools. The contraction comes from two forces: internal productivity gains from AI agents and enterprises building in-house capability centers. The new value comes from transforming business functions through human-agent operating models.

**The Nitin Seth / Fast Company analysis** frames the shift as structural, not cyclical. The consulting pyramid model — analysts gathering data, mid-level consultants synthesizing insights, partners delivering slides — is being dismantled by AI. McKinsey's internal AI platform Lilli is used by 72% of its 45,000 professionals, logging 500,000+ prompts per month and reclaiming roughly 30% of research time.

**PwC launched Agent OS (March 2025),** connecting multiple specialized AI agents under a shared governance layer. BCG launched GENE, its proprietary AI chatbot. Deloitte launched PairD and Zora AI. All major firms are simultaneously building internal AI capabilities and offering AI transformation services to clients.

**The billing model tension is acute.** The Deloitte Australia scandal crystallized the question: when AI produces the deliverable, what justifies consulting-level fees? The shift toward outcome-based pricing and subscription models (as described in HFS Research's Services-as-Software framework) represents the industry's structural response.

**Gartner predicts** 40% of enterprise applications will integrate task-specific AI agents by end of 2026, up from less than 5% in 2025. Deloitte's 2026 Software Industry Outlook projects the application software market could grow to $780B by 2030.

### 4.7 Changing Definition of Valuable Employees

**The "skill leveler" finding from the Harvard/BCG study** — where below-average performers gained 43% vs. 17% for above-average performers — has profound implications for which employees are most valuable. If AI closes the gap between junior and senior performers on routine tasks, the premium shifts to skills AI cannot replicate.

**MIT Sloan's EPOCH framework** identifies the capabilities that define post-AI employee value: empathy, perception/judgment, originality/creativity, connectedness/collaboration, and hope/vision/leadership. These are precisely the capabilities that emerge from experience, mentorship, and institutional tenure — the things that layoffs destroy.

**McKinsey's analysis of 6,800 skills in 11 million+ job postings** found 600+ new skills in demand since 2023, largely GenAI-related. But the meta-skill finding is more important: adaptability is more valuable than specialization when the ground is moving.

**Brynjolfsson's "Canaries" research** shows that early-career workers in AI-exposed occupations face the largest employment declines. This creates a paradox: the traditional apprenticeship pipeline for developing senior talent is being hollowed out at exactly the moment when senior judgment is most valuable.

## 5. Counterarguments and Tensions

### 5.1 "AI Productivity Gains Are Overstated"

**Who argues this:** Daron Acemoglu (modestly), METR researchers, various skeptics.

**Evidence:** Acemoglu's task-based model estimates total factor productivity gains of less than 0.7% cumulatively over 10 years — far below the industry hype. The METR RCT found experienced developers were 19% *slower* with AI. The 2025 Stack Overflow survey shows declining trust. BCG's own data shows only 5% of companies achieving AI value at scale.

**What the thesis may need to acknowledge:** The productivity gains from AI are real but uneven, hard to measure, and highly dependent on implementation quality. The book should not overclaim the magnitude of AI productivity gains — but it doesn't need to. The thesis works even if productivity gains are modest, because the *market* is repricing delivery labor regardless of whether individual firms capture those gains.

### 5.2 "Headcount Reduction Is Rational and Sometimes Necessary"

**Who argues this:** Corporate finance practitioners, efficiency-oriented management consultants, some labor economists.

**Evidence:** Companies that overhired during COVID are genuinely correcting. Many consulting firms' layoffs reflect market correction, not just AI displacement. In some cases, AI genuinely makes certain roles unnecessary, and forcing retention creates waste.

**What the thesis may need to acknowledge:** The book should not argue that no position should ever be eliminated. The argument is more nuanced: *defaulting* to headcount reduction as the primary response to AI productivity gains is a strategic error. Some roles will legitimately be automated. The question is what happens to the surplus capacity.

### 5.3 "The Growth Thesis Is Aspirational, Not Proven"

**Who argues this:** Implicitly, the 95% of companies that haven't achieved AI value at scale.

**Evidence:** MIT research found that 95% of gen AI projects produce zero return. Most deployments are narrow (translation, summarization) rather than transformative. The gap between pilot success and enterprise-level impact remains large.

**What the thesis may need to acknowledge:** The growth-from-AI pathway is real but difficult. Most firms will fail to execute it. The book's value proposition is in providing the playbook for the firms that want to be in the 5%.

### 5.4 "Consulting's Moat Is Relationships, Not Delivery"

**Who argues this:** Senior partners and business development leaders at consulting firms.

**Evidence:** Some truth here. Client relationships, trust, and domain expertise are genuine differentiators that AI cannot easily replicate. The premium on human judgment and relationship management may actually increase as delivery becomes commoditized.

**What the thesis may need to acknowledge:** The book should distinguish between relationship-driven advisory work (which may become *more* valuable) and delivery-driven implementation work (which faces margin compression). The strategic move is to use AI to shift the mix toward higher-value advisory.

### 5.5 Noah Smith's Critique of Acemoglu

**Who argues this:** Economist Noah Smith, in his detailed review of *Power and Progress*.

**Evidence:** Smith argues that Acemoglu and Johnson's historical analysis cherry-picks which technologies are "good" (electricity, which created jobs) vs. "bad" (textile automation, which displaced workers) without consistent criteria. He notes that the same automation technologies that immiserated British workers in the 1700s later drove prosperity in China and Bangladesh.

**What the thesis may need to acknowledge:** The automation-vs-augmentation framework is powerful but not deterministic. Context matters enormously. The book should acknowledge this complexity rather than presenting a simplistic binary.

## 6. Implications for Book Positioning

### What This Book Can Say That Existing Thought Leadership Does Not

1. **Apply the Acemoglu/Brynjolfsson frameworks specifically to the consulting firm's own business model**, not just to their clients. No existing work makes this reflexive move explicitly.

2. **Connect the organizational behavior literature on knowledge loss directly to AI-driven workforce decisions** in professional services. This is an unexploited intersection.

3. **Provide an operational framework for "growth from AI"** at the mid-market consulting firm level. The existing literature is either macro-economic theory (Acemoglu, Brynjolfsson), big-firm case studies (McKinsey, Accenture), or industry analyst projections (HFS). There is no practitioner playbook for the $50M-$500M consulting firm.

4. **Name the trap explicitly:** the instinct to use AI productivity gains for headcount reduction is the strategic equivalent of eating your seed corn. The book can coin a frame for this — something sharper than "don't automate, augment."

5. **Argue that the consulting firm that practices growth-from-AI internally becomes the most credible advisor** to clients facing the same choice. This is a positioning argument, not just a strategy argument.

### Which Claims Feel Crowded

- "AI will transform consulting" — everyone is saying this.
- "Augmentation is better than automation" — well-covered by Acemoglu, Brynjolfsson, MIT Sloan.
- "The future of consulting is outcome-based pricing" — HFS and industry analysts have this.

### Which Claims Feel Fresh

- **The reflexive application** — consulting firms need to practice what they preach.
- **The knowledge loss connection** — AI-driven layoffs in consulting specifically destroy the tacit knowledge that makes consulting valuable.
- **The mid-market playbook** — operational guidance for firms below the Big Five.
- **The "seed corn" framing** — cutting heads to capture AI gains is a one-time benefit that destroys recurring capacity.

### Recommended Positioning Angles

1. **Primary:** "The consulting firm's survival guide for the AI era — told through the lens of growth strategy, not cost reduction."
2. **Secondary:** "The book that tells consulting firm leaders to take their own medicine."

## 8. Annotated Bibliography (Top 20 Sources)

**1. Acemoglu, D. and Johnson, S. *Power and Progress: Our 1000-Year Struggle Over Technology and Prosperity.* PublicAffairs, 2023.**
The foundational text arguing that technology creates growth but the distribution of that growth depends on whether innovations augment or replace workers. Central thesis: AI is currently going in the "wrong direction" — too focused on automation and mimicking human capabilities rather than complementing them. The concept of "so-so technology" is directly relevant. Use as the intellectual anchor for the book's argument that consulting firms face the same choice.

**2. Acemoglu, D. "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI." *Economic Policy* 40(121), January 2025.**
The most rigorous economic model of AI's impact on productivity, estimating modest TFP gains (under 0.7% over 10 years) from the current automation-focused path. Important for establishing that the "AI will make everything better" narrative is economically unsupported. Use to ground the book's argument that *how* firms deploy AI matters more than whether they deploy it.

**3. Brynjolfsson, E. "The AI Productivity Take-Off Is Finally Visible." *Financial Times*, February 2026.**
The bull case for AI productivity, arguing that US productivity growth of ~2.7% in 2025 represents the beginning of the J-curve harvest phase. Important counterweight to Acemoglu's skepticism. Use to argue that the opportunity is real but requires investment in process redesign and worker training to capture.

**4. Brynjolfsson, E., Chandar, B., and Chen, R. "Canaries in the Coal Mine." Working paper, November 2025.**
Provides the most granular evidence that AI-driven employment declines are concentrated among early-career workers in automating occupations. Use to argue that the consulting industry's junior talent pipeline is at risk — a knowledge-continuity problem with long-term consequences.

**5. Dell'Acqua, F. et al. "Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier." Harvard Business School Working Paper 24-005, 2023.**
The empirical anchor for the entire augmentation argument. The "centaurs" and "cyborgs" patterns of human-AI integration provide practical models. The finding that AI applied outside its frontier *harms* performance is critical for arguing that thoughtful deployment, not wholesale automation, is the path. Use throughout the book as the evidence base for augmentation strategy.

**6. McKinsey. "Reimagining the Value Proposition of Tech Services for Agentic AI." December 2025.**
The most industry-specific projection, estimating 20-30% contraction in core tech services alongside $100-400B in new value pools. Based on a survey of 200 C-suite executives. Use as the quantified threat-and-opportunity frame for consulting firms.

**7. Seth, Nitin. "Why the McKinsey Layoffs Are a Warning Signal for Consulting in the AI Age." *Fast Company*, December 2025.**
Written by a former McKinsey Director, this provides the insider's alarm bell. The argument that the consulting pyramid model is being dismantled by AI, and that execution-centric firms are growing at double the rate of strategy-centric firms, is directly on-thesis. Use as the opening provocation or first-chapter anchor.

**8. HFS Research / Fersht, P. "Services-as-Software" framework, 2024-2025.**
The industry analyst framework most directly addressing the business model transformation. The "hours factories" label is powerful shorthand for the firms at risk. The $1.5T market projection provides scale. Use as the business model transformation framework throughout.

**9. Chen, W.X., Srinivasan, S., and Zakerinia, S. "Displacement or Complementarity?" Working paper, 2024/2025.**
Provides labor market evidence from job postings that augmentation-prone roles are growing while automation-prone roles are shrinking. The explicit recommendation that firms should treat AI as augmentation, not cost-cutting, aligns directly with the book's prescription. Use for evidence-based arguments about workforce strategy.

**10. MIT Sloan. "The EPOCH of AI: Human-Machine Complementarities at Work." March 2025.**
Provides the capability framework for identifying which employees are most strategically valuable in the AI era. The five EPOCH capabilities map directly to the book's argument about which employees to retain and develop. Use for the "changing definition of valuable employees" chapter.

**11. Becker, J. et al. (METR). "Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity." July 2025.**
The most important counterevidence. The finding that experienced developers were slower with AI challenges simplistic productivity narratives but actually strengthens the augmentation argument: implementation quality, not just adoption, determines outcomes. Use in the counterarguments chapter and as evidence that "just buy AI tools" is insufficient strategy.

**12. McKinsey. "The State of AI in 2025." November 2025.**
The broadest survey of enterprise AI adoption, providing the baseline data on how organizations are using AI. Key finding: companies that set growth and innovation (not just efficiency) as AI objectives see the most value. Use throughout for quantified benchmarks.

**13. BCG "Future-Built" Research (via Gloat, November 2025).**
The finding that only 5% of companies achieve AI at scale but those that do generate 1.7x revenue growth is powerful. The contrast between 50%+ employee upskilling at leading organizations vs. 20% at laggards provides a concrete metric. Use for the operational framework chapters.

**14. Emerald Insight. "Knowledge Loss Induced by Organizational Member Turnover." Literature review, 2023.**
The most comprehensive academic synthesis of the knowledge loss literature. Establishes that tacit knowledge loss is more harmful than explicit knowledge loss, and that the departures of critical employees produce the most damaging effects. Use to build the argument that AI-driven layoffs in consulting are especially destructive.

**15. McKinsey. "The Agentic Organization." September 2025.**
Frames the new operating paradigm as humans working alongside AI agents at near-zero marginal cost. The five pillars (business model, operating model, governance, workforce/culture, technology/data) provide a useful framework. Use for the "what does the AI-enabled consulting firm actually look like" chapter.

**16. Thoughtworks. "Service-as-Software: A New Economic Model." December 2025.**
Provides the clearest articulation of the shift from selling tools to selling outcomes. The distinction between "software that enables humans to solve problems" (SaaS) and "software that automates the reasoning process itself" (SaS) is crisp. Use for the business model chapter.

**17. Wharton Budget Model. "Projected Impact of Generative AI on Future Productivity Growth." September 2025.**
Provides the macro-economic projection that AI-exposed sectors (including professional services) will grow faster. Use to argue that consulting firms in the right position can ride a structural growth trend.

**18. Acemoglu, D. and Johnson, S. "Rebalancing AI." *IMF Finance & Development*, December 2023.**
The most accessible summary of the automation-vs-augmentation argument, written for a policy audience. The distinction between average productivity (which automation raises) and marginal productivity (which augmentation raises) is key. Use for the economic theory sections.

**19. Accenture. Growth Model Restructuring announcement. June 2025.**
The largest professional services firm restructured itself around AI in real time. The creation of "Reinvention Services" as a single integrated unit is a case study. The simultaneous layoffs (11,000) and hiring (77,000 in AI/data) illustrate the pivot. Use as a major firm case study.

**20. Anthropic/Washington Post. AI augmentation vs. automation data analysis. July 2025.**
The finding that augmentation use significantly exceeds automation use across almost all occupations, based on 1 million actual AI conversations, provides empirical grounding for the augmentation thesis from real usage data, not projections. Use for the "this is already happening" argument.

## 9. Research Gaps

### Gaps Requiring Firsthand Reporting or Interviews

1. **Mid-market consulting firm case studies.** The existing literature focuses on the Big Five (McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, Accenture) and enterprise technology. There is virtually no published evidence on how $50M-$500M consulting firms are navigating AI disruption. The book needs firsthand interviews with managing partners at this level.

2. **Client-side perspective on consulting value post-AI.** How are enterprise buyers actually changing their purchasing behavior? Are they bringing more work in-house? Demanding different pricing models? The McKinsey survey addresses this at the C-suite level, but practitioner-level evidence is thin.

3. **Knowledge loss case studies in AI-driven consulting restructuring.** The knowledge loss literature is mature, but specific examples of consulting firms losing institutional knowledge through AI-driven layoffs — and the downstream effects on client relationships — are not documented in the public literature.

4. **The mentorship pipeline problem.** Brynjolfsson's "Canaries" data shows early-career employment declining in AI-exposed occupations. What does this mean for the traditional consulting apprenticeship model? How are firms maintaining talent development when they hire fewer juniors? This requires interview-based reporting.

### Claims That Are More Conceptual Than Proven

1. **"AI-driven layoffs destroy the institutional knowledge that makes consulting valuable."** This is logically sound and supported by adjacent evidence, but no study has directly measured this effect in AI-era consulting specifically.

2. **"Firms that redirect AI productivity into growth outperform those that use it for cost reduction."** BCG's "future-built" research supports this indirectly, but the causal mechanism in consulting specifically has not been isolated.

3. **"The mid-market consulting firm is more vulnerable than the Big Five."** This is a reasonable inference (fewer resources to invest in AI, less ability to absorb transformation costs) but is not evidenced in the current literature.

### Chapters That Would Need Stronger Evidence

- Any chapter making specific ROI claims for the "growth from AI" strategy in consulting will need proprietary data or case studies.
- The "what to do with freed capacity" chapter needs operational examples — firms that actually redirected productivity gains into new services, market expansion, or capability building.
- The "which employees to retain" chapter needs more than the EPOCH framework — it needs consulting-specific profiles of high-value roles in the AI era.
