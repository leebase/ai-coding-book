
# Research Memo: Thought Leadership on AI, Consulting, and Growth

## 1. Executive Summary

- The strongest direct evidence supports the claim that AI is lowering the cost of **many bounded software-development and knowledge-work tasks**, not all software work. Experimental and field evidence shows large gains in coding speed or output in some settings, including [Peng et al. (2023)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590), [Cui et al. (2025)](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf), and the broader synthesis in [Stanford HAI’s 2025 AI Index](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2025-ai-index-report).
- The evidence is **heterogeneous rather than universal**. In mature, context-heavy repositories, experienced open-source developers were **19% slower** with early-2025 AI tools in METR’s randomized study ([METR, 2025](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/); paper: [PDF](https://metr.org/Early_2025_AI_Experienced_OS_Devs_Study-paper.pdf)). The HBS/BCG “jagged frontier” work likewise shows that AI helps strongly inside its competence boundary and can hurt outside it ([Dell’Acqua et al., 2023](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700); [BCG, 2023](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai)).
- The labor effect looks more like **skill compression and augmentation** than instant wholesale replacement. AI often helps less-experienced or lower-performing workers the most, as shown in [Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161), [Cui et al. (2025)](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf), and [BCG’s GPT-4 experiment with consultants (2023)](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai).
- There is real evidence that firms expect to use AI for **headcount reduction**, but the same sources also show strong parallel plans for **upskilling and redeployment**. The clearest survey evidence comes from the [World Economic Forum’s Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/) and [McKinsey’s 2025 AI work](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai), while [PwC’s 2025 AI Jobs Barometer](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf) argues that AI exposure is also associated with faster productivity and wage growth.
- The claim that layoffs or over-optimization can destroy tacit knowledge and continuity is **well grounded in older management and organizational research**, though it is usually not AI-specific. Key evidence includes [Galan’s 2023 systematic review on turnover-driven knowledge loss](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S0969647423000551), [Hong’s 2025 study of turnover and delays in the federal IT workforce](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/35/4/434/8162522), [Trevor and Nyberg (2008)](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/amj.2008.31767250), [Brockner et al. (1992)](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/256380), [Datta et al. (2010)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0149206309346735), and [Subramony and Holtom (2012)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1094670512452792).
- The most forward-looking management literature does **not** stop at efficiency. It argues that durable AI value comes from **workflow redesign, complementary investments, new offerings, and employee empowerment**, not just labor takeout. The strongest sources here are [Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson’s productivity J-curve](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148), [McKinsey’s Superagency report (2025)](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work), [McKinsey’s State of AI 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai), [McKinsey’s 2025 innovation piece](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-next-innovation-revolution-powered-by-ai), [PwC’s 2025 AI Jobs Barometer](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf), and [BCG’s AI at Work 2025](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain).
- Analyst and consulting-firm commentary strongly suggests that AI will pressure the **time-and-materials, pyramid, and labor-arbitrage economics** of many professional-services businesses. The clearest sources are [Forrester (2025)](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/), [KPMG UK (2025)](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/uk/pdf/2025/06/client-centric-future.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf), [HFS Research on Services-as-Software™ (2025)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/), [HFS on software/services collision (2025)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/service-software-collision-age-old/), [Everest Group (2025)](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/), and [Source Global Research’s 2026 market update](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth).
- Buyer-side and market commentary **does not** support a simplistic “consulting is dead” thesis. It points instead to **value migration**: more pressure on generic delivery hours, more willingness to pay for AI-enabled speed and outcomes, and more demand for firms that can orchestrate transformation credibly. Useful evidence comes from [Source Global Research (2024)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/our-most-viewed-charts-of-2024-how-have-things-changed), [Source Global Research (2026)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth), [Source’s ‘Probably not’ counterargument (2026)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/will-the-efficiency-gains-from-ai-destroy-the-professional-services-industry-probably-not), [Accenture’s 2025 shareholder letter](https://investor.accenture.com/~/media/Files/A/accenture-v4/investors/home/accenture-2025-letter-to-shareholders.pdf), and [PwC One (2026)](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/pwc-one.html).
- The **freshest and least crowded part** of the proposed thesis is the argument that AI-era firms can damage long-term growth when they convert productivity gains into blunt headcount cuts and thereby liquidate tacit knowledge, client memory, apprenticeship capacity, and continuity. Existing literature supports each piece separately, but the integrated argument is not yet well developed in one place.
- Overall assessment: the thesis is **partially supported overall and novel in synthesis**. The component claims around AI productivity, consulting-model pressure, augmentation, and the organizational cost of poorly managed cuts are strong. The full causal claim that AI-era layoffs in consulting will reliably weaken long-term growth is plausible and well-motivated, but still needs more consulting-specific, longitudinal evidence.

## 2. Core Thesis Framing

### Cleaner restatement of the thesis

A sharper version of the book’s argument would be:

> As AI compresses the unit economics of routine coding, implementation, research, and other standardized delivery work, consulting firms that still monetize delivery labor primarily by the hour face growing price pressure and commoditization. The defensible strategy is not indiscriminate headcount reduction, but using AI-enabled productivity to redesign work, protect tacit client and organizational knowledge, create new offerings, and shift the business model toward outcomes, platforms, and growth.

That framing is more defensible than a blanket claim that “software delivery no longer matters.” The literature supports a narrower but stronger version: **routine, well-specified, and repeatable delivery work is under the most pressure** ([Peng et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590); [Cui et al., 2025](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf); [METR, 2025](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/); [Dell’Acqua et al., 2023](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700)).

### Strongest adjacent ideas already in the market

Several strong adjacent ideas are already established:

- **Professional-services leverage economics**. Long before generative AI, [David Maister’s classic work](https://davidmaister.com/books.mtpsf/) explained that consulting economics depend on leverage, apprenticeship, and the surplus from junior labor. His related essay on the anatomy of consulting firms explicitly ties profitability to the mix of “finders, minders and grinders” and to the ability to bill junior time at a markup ([Maister’s article](https://davidmaister.com/articles/16/2/)).
- **Technology as a challenge to professional gatekeeping**. [Richard and Daniel Susskind’s *The Future of the Professions* (2015)](https://www.danielsusskind.com/the-future-of-the-professions) frames professional work as facing two futures: a more efficient version of current delivery, or gradual replacement of parts of professional expertise by increasingly capable systems.
- **The productivity J-curve and complementary investment thesis**. [Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson (2018/2021)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148) argue that general-purpose technologies create value only after organizational complements are built. This is a major adjacent idea to the book’s “productivity into growth” claim.
- **The jagged technological frontier**. [Dell’Acqua et al. (2023)](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700) and [BCG’s companion write-up](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai) show that AI improves performance unevenly by task type and can make people worse when they operate beyond the model’s core capabilities.
- **Employee empowerment rather than top-down control**. [McKinsey’s Superagency report (2025)](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work) argues that the main bottleneck is leadership and organizational redesign, not employee willingness to use AI.
- **Services-as-Software / non-linear growth**. [HFS Research (2025)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/) argues that the services sector is beginning to adopt software-like economics, with revenue and margin growth becoming less tightly coupled to headcount.
- **Pricing and proposition redesign**. [KPMG UK (2025)](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/uk/pdf/2025/06/client-centric-future.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf), [Forrester (2025)](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/), [Everest Group (2025)](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/), and [Source Global Research (2026)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth) all argue, from different angles, that firms will need to move away from pure time-based billing and generic staffing pyramids.

### What seems original about this angle

What looks genuinely original is **not** the claim that AI changes software work. That claim is already crowded.

What appears more distinctive is the combination of four ideas that are usually discussed separately:

1. **AI compresses the economics of routine delivery work** in consulting and software-heavy services.
2. **The conventional response—cost takeout and headcount reduction—can destroy the tacit, relational, and apprenticeship capital** that knowledge-intensive firms need for continuity and growth.
3. **AI changes who is strategically valuable**, not just how many people are needed: context-holders, architects, integrators, change leaders, and teachers may become scarcer and more important even as some junior or repetitive work shrinks.
4. The firms that win will be those that convert productivity into **growth, new offerings, capability transfer, and better client outcomes**, rather than treating AI as a one-time labor-efficiency event.

That integrated frame is not yet cleanly owned by any one major thinker or firm. It is therefore a viable book angle, provided the book is careful to label where the evidence is direct and where it is inference.



## 3. Key Thinkers and Institutions

- **David Maister**
  - **Why they matter:** Maister remains the clearest foundational writer on the economics of professional-service firms. His work is still the best baseline for understanding why the classic consulting pyramid works.
  - **Relevant argument:** Consulting profits depend on leverage: a mix of senior rainmakers/managers and junior delivery staff, plus an apprenticeship ladder that turns juniors into future client leaders ([Managing the Professional Service Firm](https://davidmaister.com/books.mtpsf/); [The Anatomy of a Consulting Firm](https://davidmaister.com/articles/16/2/)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports** the thesis indirectly. If AI compresses the value of routine “grinder” work, then it destabilizes the economic and developmental logic of the classic pyramid.
  - **Key sources:** [Book page](https://davidmaister.com/books.mtpsf/); [Article](https://davidmaister.com/articles/16/2/).

- **Richard and Daniel Susskind**
  - **Why they matter:** They are major long-run thinkers on how technology changes expert work and professional gatekeeping.
  - **Relevant argument:** Professional work has two plausible futures: one more efficient version of the old model, and one gradual replacement of pieces of professional expertise by systems ([The Future of the Professions (2015)](https://www.danielsusskind.com/the-future-of-the-professions)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports and complicates**. They support the broader commoditization risk, but their frame is wider than consulting and does not center growth-through-redesign.
  - **Key sources:** [Official book page](https://www.danielsusskind.com/the-future-of-the-professions).

- **Erik Brynjolfsson and collaborators**
  - **Why they matter:** Brynjolfsson’s work is among the strongest empirical evidence that generative AI can raise productivity while changing skill distributions and organizational design requirements.
  - **Relevant argument:** Generative AI often improves productivity most for less-experienced workers, can reduce attrition, and only pays off fully when paired with organizational complements ([Generative AI at Work](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [NBER digest summary](https://www.nber.org/digest/20236/measuring-productivity-impact-generative-ai); [The Productivity J-Curve](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the augmentation, skill-compression, and “convert productivity into redesign” parts of the thesis.
  - **Key sources:** [Brynjolfsson et al. (2023)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [J-curve paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148).

- **David Autor**
  - **Why they matter:** Autor is a leading labor economist whose recent AI writing is explicitly about using AI to broaden expertise instead of simply replacing labor.
  - **Relevant argument:** AI can reconfigure work to make more people capable of higher-value tasks, potentially rebuilding middle-skill work rather than hollowing it out ([Applying AI to Rebuild Middle Class Jobs (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports** the “augment employees instead of replace them” strand and gives it a serious labor-economics foundation.
  - **Key sources:** [Autor (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140).

- **Daron Acemoglu**
  - **Why they matter:** Acemoglu is the strongest mainstream counterweight to maximalist AI-productivity claims.
  - **Relevant argument:** Near-term AI gains may be concentrated in easier tasks and therefore yield only modest macro gains at first ([The Simple Macroeconomics of AI (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Complicates** the thesis. He does not negate commoditization pressure in selected tasks, but he cautions against assuming economy-wide transformation will be immediate or uniform.
  - **Key sources:** [Acemoglu (2024)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487).

- **Fabrizio Dell’Acqua, Karim Lakhani, Ethan Mollick, and the HBS/BCG research stream**
  - **Why they matter:** This is one of the most influential bodies of research on AI’s uneven effects on knowledge work.
  - **Relevant argument:** AI is powerful but jagged: it boosts productivity and quality inside certain task boundaries while creating overconfidence and lower quality outside them ([HBS working paper](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700); [BCG article](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports and sharpens** the thesis. It suggests that commoditization will hit the most standardizable parts of consulting and delivery first, while work requiring judgment and frontier navigation may become more valuable.
  - **Key sources:** [Dell’Acqua et al. (2023)](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700); [BCG summary](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai).

- **Kevin Cui, Mert Demirer, and coauthors**
  - **Why they matter:** Their three-company field experiment is among the strongest direct studies of generative AI in real software-development environments.
  - **Relevant argument:** AI increased average output for developers across Microsoft, Accenture, and a Fortune 100 firm, but gains varied by task type and developer experience ([Cui et al. (2025)](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the claim that parts of software delivery are getting cheaper, especially for less-experienced developers.
  - **Key sources:** [Working paper PDF](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf).

- **METR**
  - **Why they matter:** METR provides one of the most important recent counterexamples to blanket claims about AI making software engineers uniformly faster.
  - **Relevant argument:** In realistic, high-context open-source work on familiar repositories, experienced developers were slower with AI tools ([METR blog summary](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/); [paper](https://metr.org/Early_2025_AI_Experienced_OS_Devs_Study-paper.pdf)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Contradicts and improves** the thesis. The book should argue that AI commoditizes some delivery tasks, not all software engineering.
  - **Key sources:** [METR summary](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/); [PDF](https://metr.org/Early_2025_AI_Experienced_OS_Devs_Study-paper.pdf).

- **McKinsey / QuantumBlack**
  - **Why they matter:** McKinsey has published one of the broadest ongoing research streams on enterprise AI adoption, workflow redesign, employee sentiment, and realized value.
  - **Relevant argument:** Organizations that capture the most AI value redesign workflows, target innovation as well as efficiency, and close the gap between employee adoption and leadership behavior ([Superagency (2025)](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work); [State of AI 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai); [Rewiring to capture value (2025)](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value); [The next innovation revolution (2025)](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-next-innovation-revolution-powered-by-ai)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the “empower people and redesign work” portion of the thesis, while also reminding the author that enterprise EBIT impact is still uneven.
  - **Key sources:** [Superagency](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work); [State of AI 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai).

- **PwC**
  - **Why they matter:** PwC is important both as a major consulting firm and as a publisher of labor-market and professional-services commentary.
  - **Relevant argument:** PwC’s 2025 AI Jobs Barometer argues that AI-exposed industries are seeing faster productivity and wage growth and explicitly warns leaders to treat AI as a growth strategy, not just an efficiency strategy. Its 2026 professional-services messaging also emphasizes that AI should amplify, not replace, judgment ([AI Jobs Barometer (2025)](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf); [PwC One (2026)](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/pwc-one.html); [PwC’s marketing analogy (2025)](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/consulting/front-office/marketing-in-the-ai-era/to-matter-more-or-cost-less.html)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the growth-over-cost-only framing and the claim that consultancies can become more valuable if they help clients redesign work well.
  - **Key sources:** [AI Jobs Barometer](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf); [PwC One](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/pwc-one.html).

- **Accenture**
  - **Why they matter:** Accenture is both a direct participant in one of the best developer-productivity studies and one of the clearest examples of a large consultancy repositioning around AI.
  - **Relevant argument:** The firm is investing heavily in AI, training large parts of its workforce, and reporting billions in AI bookings and revenue while talking explicitly about reinventing both what it sells and how it delivers ([Accenture 2025 shareholder letter](https://investor.accenture.com/~/media/Files/A/accenture-v4/investors/home/accenture-2025-letter-to-shareholders.pdf)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports** the claim that large firms expect consulting to change materially, but **complicates** any simplistic “AI kills consulting” argument because incumbents are actively moving up the value chain.
  - **Key sources:** [Letter to shareholders](https://investor.accenture.com/~/media/Files/A/accenture-v4/investors/home/accenture-2025-letter-to-shareholders.pdf).

- **KPMG**
  - **Why they matter:** KPMG’s professional-services report is one of the clearest consulting-firm statements that pricing and propositions are changing.
  - **Relevant argument:** Clients want better outputs at lower rates, talent costs are rising, and firms need to move away from traditional time-based billing toward value-based propositions ([Client centric future of professional services (2025)](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/uk/pdf/2025/06/client-centric-future.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the margin-compression and business-model-shift parts of the thesis.
  - **Key sources:** [KPMG UK report](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/uk/pdf/2025/06/client-centric-future.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf).

- **Ted Schadler / Forrester**
  - **Why they matter:** Forrester provides perhaps the closest analyst-language match to the book’s core argument.
  - **Relevant argument:** AI delivery platforms let consultants do more at lower cost, which pressures margins, reduces the need for headcount-led scaling, and forces changes in pricing, staffing, and delivery platforms ([Forrester blog, 2025](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Very strongly supports** the claim that coding-heavy and implementation-heavy consulting models are vulnerable.
  - **Key sources:** [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/).

- **HFS Research**
  - **Why they matter:** HFS is one of the most explicit analyst voices on “services-as-software” and the shift from labor-driven to tech-arbitrage-driven services economics.
  - **Relevant argument:** Service providers can create non-linear growth through platforms, software, and AI; software firms are increasingly packaging intelligence that once required consultants; and firms that underinvest in people may pay more later ([Services-as-Software™ (2025)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/); [software/services collision (2025)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/service-software-collision-age-old/); [invest in your people (2024)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/pay-more-need-to-for-ai/); [choose your AI future (2025)](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/choose-your-ai-future/)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the model-shift claim and the people-investment claim.
  - **Key sources:** [HFS Services-as-Software](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/); [HFS collision course](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/service-software-collision-age-old/).

- **Everest Group**
  - **Why they matter:** Everest is useful as a buyer-side and provider-selection analyst, not just a pundit on technology.
  - **Relevant argument:** Buyers increasingly prefer providers that can show advanced AI capability, relevant experience, and value-based pricing structures such as outcome-based or gain-sharing contracts ([Everest Group, 2025](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports** the idea that consulting value will migrate away from hours and toward demonstrable outcomes and expertise.
  - **Key sources:** [Everest Group report page](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/).

- **Source Global Research**
  - **Why they matter:** Source is especially useful because it tracks client sentiment and consulting-market demand directly.
  - **Relevant argument:** Source sees persistent price pressure, rising client expectations around AI-enabled delivery, continued willingness to buy AI-related consulting, and increasing pressure on the bottom layer of the staffing pyramid ([Our most-viewed charts of 2024](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/our-most-viewed-charts-of-2024-how-have-things-changed); [Will AI be a blockbuster consulting service? (2025)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/will-ai-be-a-blockbuster-consulting-service-and-what-can-firms-do-to-influence-this); [2026 market update](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth); [‘Probably not’ counterargument (2026)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/will-the-efficiency-gains-from-ai-destroy-the-professional-services-industry-probably-not)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports and tempers**. Source supports the shift in value but argues against apocalyptic claims about consulting’s demise.
  - **Key sources:** [Source 2024](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/our-most-viewed-charts-of-2024-how-have-things-changed); [Source 2026](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth).

- **World Economic Forum**
  - **Why they matter:** WEF remains one of the best large-scale sources on how employers themselves expect AI to affect workforce structure.
  - **Relevant argument:** Employers are simultaneously planning staff reductions, upskilling, redeployment, and hiring for new skills ([Future of Jobs 2025 digest](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/); [WEF press release, 2025](https://www.weforum.org/press/2025/01/future-of-jobs-report-2025-78-million-new-job-opportunities-by-2030-but-urgent-upskilling-needed-to-prepare-workforces/)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports** the claim that headcount reduction is a common managerial response, but **complicates** any argument that reduction is the only response.
  - **Key sources:** [WEF digest](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/); [WEF press release](https://www.weforum.org/press/2025/01/future-of-jobs-report-2025-78-million-new-job-opportunities-by-2030-but-urgent-upskilling-needed-to-prepare-workforces/).

- **Deloitte and Gartner**
  - **Why they matter:** These institutions are useful for the “which humans matter most?” question.
  - **Relevant argument:** Deloitte emphasizes reskilling, human capabilities, coaching, apprenticeship, and scarce specialized talent ([future of work and AI](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/about/future-of-work-ai.html); [tech talent churn](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/tech-talent-ai-strategy.html); [high-performing teams in the AI era](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html)). Gartner reports that CIOs expect most future IT work to be **human work with AI**, not AI alone ([Gartner, 2025](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-20-gartner-survey-finds-all-it-work-will-involve-ai-by-2030-organizations-must-navigate-ai-readiness-and-human-readiness-to-find-capture-and-sustain-value)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Supports** the argument that AI changes the definition of valuable employees instead of simply making people irrelevant.
  - **Key sources:** [Deloitte future of work](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/about/future-of-work-ai.html); [Deloitte teams](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html); [Gartner](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-20-gartner-survey-finds-all-it-work-will-involve-ai-by-2030-organizations-must-navigate-ai-readiness-and-human-readiness-to-find-capture-and-sustain-value).

- **Knowledge-loss and downsizing scholars**
  - **Why they matter:** This literature is the backbone for the thesis’s warning about cuts destroying continuity and memory.
  - **Relevant argument:** Turnover causes knowledge loss; embedded organizational memory can buffer some of the damage; downsizing can trigger additional voluntary turnover; layoffs can hurt survivor effort; and attrition can damage customer outcomes and profits ([Galan, 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S0969647423000551); [Hong, 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/35/4/434/8162522); [Trevor and Nyberg, 2008](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/amj.2008.31767250); [Brockner et al., 1992](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/256380); [Datta et al., 2010](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0149206309346735); [Subramony and Holtom, 2012](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1094670512452792)).
  - **Relation to the thesis:** **Strongly supports** the organizational-risk argument, while also highlighting a limitation: the literature is mostly about turnover and downsizing in general, not AI-specific consulting layoffs.
  - **Key sources:** [Galan](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S0969647423000551); [Hong](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/35/4/434/8162522); [Subramony and Holtom](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1094670512452792).



## 4. Evidence by Theme

### AI reduces the cost of software delivery

**What the literature directly shows.** The strongest direct evidence says that generative AI can materially reduce effort on **specific** coding tasks. In a widely cited controlled study, developers using GitHub Copilot completed a task **55.8% faster** than those without it ([Peng et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590)). A more important piece of evidence for this book is the three-company field experiment by [Cui et al. (2025)](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf), which studied 4,867 developers at Microsoft, Accenture, and a Fortune 100 company. That paper reports meaningful gains in completed tasks, commits, and builds, with larger benefits for less-experienced developers. The broader synthesis in [Stanford HAI’s 2025 AI Index](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2025-ai-index-report) concludes that the evidence base for AI-driven productivity improvement in business work materially widened in 2024–2025.

**Where the evidence is thinner or contradictory.** The empirical story becomes weaker as tasks become more open-ended, context-dependent, or deeply embedded in existing systems. [METR’s 2025 study](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/) found that experienced open-source developers working on their own familiar repositories were slower with early-2025 AI tools. The [HBS working paper on the jagged technological frontier](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700) and [BCG’s write-up](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai) show a similar pattern in knowledge work: AI increases performance when tasks fit the model’s strengths, but can degrade performance beyond that frontier. [McKinsey’s March 2025 State of AI report](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value) also notes a practical translation problem: use of generative AI is spreading into software engineering, but most organizations still do not report enterprise-level EBIT impact.

**Reasonable inference for the book.** The best-supported claim is not “AI makes software cheap” in general. It is narrower and stronger: **AI is compressing the cost of routine, specifiable, and reviewable software-delivery work**, especially where the work product can be rapidly tested, compared, and refined. That is exactly the portion of delivery work that consulting firms have historically staffed with large numbers of juniors and monetized by the hour. The book should therefore talk about the commoditization of **slices** of delivery, not the death of engineering or implementation writ large.

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- Routine coding and implementation tasks are becoming cheaper in many settings ([Peng et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590); [Cui et al., 2025](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf)).
- The largest gains often accrue to less-experienced developers, which matters for staffing pyramids ([Cui et al., 2025](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf); [Brynjolfsson et al., 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161)).
- Mature, high-context, exception-heavy engineering work is much less clearly commoditized ([METR, 2025](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/); [Acemoglu, 2024](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487)).
- The book should target “routine delivery economics” rather than all software work ([HBS/BCG jagged frontier research](https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700); [BCG, 2023](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai)).

### AI and headcount reduction

**What the literature directly shows.** There is clear evidence that many employers expect AI to change workforce size. The [World Economic Forum’s Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/) reports that **40%** of employers plan to reduce staff where skills become less relevant, while **50%** plan to transition people from declining roles into growing roles and **85%** plan to prioritize upskilling. The accompanying [WEF press release](https://www.weforum.org/press/2025/01/future-of-jobs-report-2025-78-million-new-job-opportunities-by-2030-but-urgent-upskilling-needed-to-prepare-workforces/) presents a similar picture and reports that **41%** of employers intend to reduce workforce where AI can automate tasks. [McKinsey’s State of AI 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) likewise reports that some organizations expect workforce decreases from AI adoption, but many expect no change and a minority expect increases. In other words, reduction is real, but not the only observed response.

**Concrete case evidence.** [Reuters’ 2024 Klarna report](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/swedens-klarna-says-ai-chatbots-help-shrink-headcount-2024-08-27/) is a vivid example of AI-first cost logic in practice: the company described AI chatbots as enabling substantial headcount shrinkage and highlighted higher revenue per employee. But [Reuters’ 2025 follow-up](https://www.reuters.com/business/swedens-klarna-shifts-ai-focus-cost-cuts-growth-2025-09-10/) is just as important for the book’s argument, because it suggests that an AI strategy centered too narrowly on labor reduction can run into quality and growth limits.

**Reasonable inference for the book.** It is fair to say that **many firms will instinctively attempt to cash out AI gains through labor reduction**, especially in functions that already feel overstaffed or commoditized. It is not fair to say that the research supports a simple one-way story toward mass unemployment. The better-supported claim is that AI creates a managerial fork in the road: reduction, redeployment, or redesign—and firms are trying some combination of all three ([WEF, 2025](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/); [McKinsey, 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai); [PwC, 2025](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf)).

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- A headcount-reduction instinct is clearly present in employer surveys ([WEF digest](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/); [WEF press release](https://www.weforum.org/press/2025/01/future-of-jobs-report-2025-78-million-new-job-opportunities-by-2030-but-urgent-upskilling-needed-to-prepare-workforces/)).
- Redeployment and upskilling appear just as central to employer plans as reduction ([WEF digest](https://www.weforum.org/publications/the-future-of-jobs-report-2025/digest/); [McKinsey Superagency](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work)).
- Case evidence like Klarna shows how seductive AI-led efficiency narratives can be ([Reuters, 2024](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/swedens-klarna-says-ai-chatbots-help-shrink-headcount-2024-08-27/)).
- The book should frame reduction as a **common managerial response**, not as an inevitable labor-market outcome ([Humlum and Vestergaard, 2025](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33777)).

### augmentation vs replacement

**What the literature directly shows.** The most rigorous evidence still tilts toward **augmentation**. [Brynjolfsson, Li, and Raymond (2023)](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161) found about a **14%** productivity increase in customer support overall and roughly **35%** gains for the least-skilled or least-experienced workers, with little or no gain for top performers. Their NBER digest summary also notes lower worker attrition in the AI-enabled condition ([NBER digest](https://www.nber.org/digest/20236/measuring-productivity-impact-generative-ai)). [David Autor’s 2024 paper](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140) gives the strongest theoretical-economic case for using AI to extend expertise rather than eliminate people. [PwC’s 2025 AI Jobs Barometer](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf) reinforces this view at market scale: it argues that AI-exposed industries are seeing faster productivity growth, faster wage growth, and job growth even in categories often treated as highly automatable.

**Why replacement remains a live issue.** Replacement is not imaginary; it is just uneven. The [BCG/HBS frontier research](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai) shows that lower performers gain more from AI, which can flatten differences between average and elite workers on some tasks. That can reduce the scarcity value of routine expertise. [Gartner’s 2025 survey of CIOs](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-20-gartner-survey-finds-all-it-work-will-involve-ai-by-2030-organizations-must-navigate-ai-readiness-and-human-readiness-to-find-capture-and-sustain-value) adds an important frame: by 2030, CIOs expect all IT work to involve AI, but they expect the largest share to be **human work augmented by AI**, not AI-only work. [Deloitte’s future-of-work page](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/about/future-of-work-ai.html) and [Deloitte’s 2026 high-performing-teams release](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html) make a similar point: human skills, coaching, trust, and team design matter more, not less, in AI-intensive environments.

**Reasonable inference for the book.** The better thesis is: **AI replaces tasks faster than it replaces jobs, and it changes the value mix inside jobs faster than it eliminates whole occupations.** In consulting, that means more pressure on templated research, analysis, code generation, and standard implementation steps; less pressure on client framing, messy transformation work, political navigation, governance, and judgment.

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- The best evidence supports augmentation more strongly than wholesale job replacement ([Brynjolfsson et al., 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [Autor, 2024](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140); [PwC, 2025](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf)).
- AI especially lifts lower performers or less-experienced workers, which compresses parts of the skill hierarchy ([Brynjolfsson et al., 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [BCG, 2023](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai); [Cui et al., 2025](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf)).
- Human-plus-AI work appears more likely than AI-only work in mainstream IT and knowledge settings ([Gartner, 2025](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-20-gartner-survey-finds-all-it-work-will-involve-ai-by-2030-organizations-must-navigate-ai-readiness-and-human-readiness-to-find-capture-and-sustain-value); [Deloitte, 2026](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html)).
- For the book, the key distinction is between **task substitution** and **capability redesign**.

### knowledge loss, continuity, and tacit knowledge

**What the literature directly shows.** This is one of the strongest parts of the proposed thesis—but the evidence comes mostly from organizational research rather than AI-specific studies. [Nataliya Galan’s 2023 review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S0969647423000551) synthesizes 91 empirical studies on turnover-driven knowledge loss and documents both the antecedents of knowledge loss and the mechanisms organizations use to cope with it. [Ji Hyeun Hong’s 2025 study](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/35/4/434/8162522) is especially valuable for this project because it is directly about the federal IT workforce: turnover was associated with project delays, while organizational memory embedded in structures such as centralization and standardization reduced the damage. [Trevor and Nyberg (2008)](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/amj.2008.31767250) show that downsizing can predict higher voluntary turnover, meaning cuts can cause the wrong people to leave. [Brockner et al. (1992)](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/256380) show that survivor effort can deteriorate when insecurity becomes too high. [Datta et al. (2010)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0149206309346735) reviews a broad body of downsizing research and emphasizes that firm-level efficiency logic often misses longer-run organizational and human costs.

**Why this matters especially in services.** [Subramony and Holtom (2012)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1094670512452792) is particularly useful because it links attrition and downsizing to customer outcomes and future profitability through disruption of relational assets. That is highly relevant to consulting, where value often resides not just in codified deliverables but in shared context, informal know-how, internal coordination routines, and trusted client relationships. [David Maister’s classic framing](https://davidmaister.com/articles/16/2/) reinforces the point from a professional-services perspective: consulting firms are also apprenticeship systems. Cutting too deeply can damage both current delivery continuity and future partner-development capacity.

**A necessary nuance.** The book should not overstate this evidence. The literature proves that turnover and downsizing can destroy knowledge, continuity, and customer value. It does **not** yet directly prove that AI-motivated layoffs in consulting always do so. There is also an important counterpoint: [McKinsey’s 2025 innovation piece](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-next-innovation-revolution-powered-by-ai) argues that AI can help organizations codify some previously tacit knowledge from meetings, communications, and expert workflows. That means AI may mitigate some knowledge loss—but almost certainly not all of it, especially relational and client-specific knowledge.

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- Knowledge-intensive firms face real knowledge-loss risk when people leave ([Galan, 2023](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S0969647423000551)).
- In IT and project-based work, turnover can slow execution unless organizational memory is deliberately embedded ([Hong, 2025](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/35/4/434/8162522)).
- Downsizing can trigger second-order losses through survivor effects and additional voluntary exits ([Trevor and Nyberg, 2008](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/amj.2008.31767250); [Brockner et al., 1992](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/256380)).
- In service businesses, attrition can damage customer outcomes and profit, not just staffing levels ([Subramony and Holtom, 2012](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1094670512452792)).
- The book should talk about the risks of **indiscriminate or over-optimized cuts**, not claim that every reduction is self-defeating.

### productivity redirected into growth

**What the literature directly shows.** The clearest conceptual source here is [Brynjolfsson, Rock, and Syverson’s productivity J-curve](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148), which argues that general-purpose technologies create larger value only after complementary investments in process, organization, and intangible capital. In practical AI terms, [McKinsey’s State of AI 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai) reports that organizations seeing the greatest value from AI are not only reducing costs but also pursuing innovation and transformation. [McKinsey’s March 2025 report](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value) adds an important reality check: gen-AI use is widespread, but large-scale financial impact still depends on rewiring workflows. [McKinsey’s June 2025 innovation article](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-next-innovation-revolution-powered-by-ai) makes the point even more starkly: “AI isn’t just for efficiency anymore,” especially in R&D and innovation settings.

**The strongest non-McKinsey support.** [PwC’s 2025 AI Jobs Barometer](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf) is unusually helpful because it explicitly says leaders should treat AI as a **growth strategy**, not only an efficiency strategy. [PwC’s 2025 marketing essay](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/consulting/front-office/marketing-in-the-ai-era/to-matter-more-or-cost-less.html) gives a language frame that fits this book very well: organizations can use AI either to “matter more” or merely to “cost less.” [BCG’s AI at Work 2025](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain) similarly emphasizes the redesign of people-machine collaboration, workflows, and measurement systems rather than narrow cost takeout.

**Concrete case evidence.** The [Klarna 2024 Reuters story](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/swedens-klarna-says-ai-chatbots-help-shrink-headcount-2024-08-27/) captures the initial attraction of cost reduction. The [2025 Reuters follow-up](https://www.reuters.com/business/swedens-klarna-shifts-ai-focus-cost-cuts-growth-2025-09-10/) captures the pivot toward growth and service quality. One case does not prove a general law, but it is a vivid illustration of the book’s core managerial fork: use AI to shrink, or use it to expand capability and demand.

**Reasonable inference for the book.** The strongest version of the argument is that **AI productivity gains should be treated as investable strategic slack**. Firms can spend that slack on deeper client work, faster innovation, new products, better customer experience, and internal capability building. If they spend it only on labor removal, they may get a short-term efficiency win while sacrificing the compounding effects that make knowledge-intensive firms valuable.

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- Productivity gains do not automatically become enterprise value; they require complementary redesign ([J-curve](https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148); [McKinsey, March 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value)).
- High-performing AI adopters are aiming at innovation and transformation, not just cost takeout ([McKinsey, Nov. 2025](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai); [BCG, 2025](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain)).
- PwC offers unusually direct support for the “growth from productivity” framing ([PwC, 2025](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf); [PwC marketing essay, 2025](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/consulting/front-office/marketing-in-the-ai-era/to-matter-more-or-cost-less.html)).
- The book should present cost savings as the shallowest use of AI, not the highest-value use.

### future of consulting and professional services

**What the literature directly shows.** The direct evidence here is mostly analyst commentary, consulting-firm reports, and market observation rather than academic causal study. Even so, the pattern is strikingly consistent. [Forrester (2025)](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/) argues that AI delivery platforms let consultants do more work at lower cost, which pressures margins and pushes firms toward new staffing models, repricing, and platform investment. [KPMG UK (2025)](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/uk/pdf/2025/06/client-centric-future.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf) says clients expect higher-quality outputs at lower rates and that pricing and propositions need to be reimagined away from traditional time-based billing. [HFS Research’s Services-as-Software™ thesis](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/) argues that service providers can achieve non-linear growth by embedding software and AI into delivery. [HFS’s software/services collision piece](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/service-software-collision-age-old/) says software vendors are increasingly packaging intelligence that once required consulting labor.

**What enterprise buyers and market trackers are saying.** [Everest Group (2025)](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/) reports that buyers prefer providers with advanced expertise, relevant experience, and value-driven pricing such as outcome-based or gain-sharing models. [Source Global Research (2024)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/our-most-viewed-charts-of-2024-how-have-things-changed) reports intense price pressure but also client willingness to pay more when firms use AI-enabled tools well. [Source’s 2026 market update](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth) is especially close to the proposed thesis: it argues that AI-based research may replace the bottom layer of the staffing pyramid, increase outcome-based pricing, and require more investment in both people and machines. At the same time, [Source’s February 2026 counterpoint](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/will-the-efficiency-gains-from-ai-destroy-the-professional-services-industry-probably-not) says the efficiency gains from AI are unlikely to destroy the professional-services industry outright.

**How the major firms are positioning themselves.** [Accenture’s 2025 shareholder letter](https://investor.accenture.com/~/media/Files/A/accenture-v4/investors/home/accenture-2025-letter-to-shareholders.pdf) portrays AI as a large revenue stream and a reinvention of delivery. [PwC One (2026)](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/pwc-one.html) portrays professional services as becoming more continuous, insight-rich, and AI-enabled while keeping human judgment central. These are obviously firm perspectives rather than neutral evidence, but they matter because they show how incumbents themselves understand the shift.

**Reasonable inference for the book.** The best-supported market thesis is: **AI will not erase consulting; it will put generic delivery economics under pressure and push value toward platforms, outcomes, domain expertise, orchestration, governance, and transformation.** That is not an apocalyptic thesis. It is a migration-of-value thesis.

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- Time-based billing and junior-heavy leverage models are under growing pressure ([KPMG, 2025](https://assets.kpmg.com/content/dam/kpmgsites/uk/pdf/2025/06/client-centric-future.pdf.coredownload.inline.pdf); [Forrester, 2025](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/); [Source, 2026](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth)).
- AI lets providers deliver faster and cheaper, which undermines pure labor-hour monetization ([Forrester, 2025](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/); [HFS, 2025](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/)).
- Buyers still want consulting support, but increasingly on the basis of outcomes, expertise, and shared value ([Everest, 2025](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/); [Source, 2025](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/will-ai-be-a-blockbuster-consulting-service-and-what-can-firms-do-to-influence-this)).
- The strongest consulting-firm response is not denial but reinvention ([Accenture, 2025](https://investor.accenture.com/~/media/Files/A/accenture-v4/investors/home/accenture-2025-letter-to-shareholders.pdf); [PwC One, 2026](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/pwc-one.html)).

### changing definition of valuable employees

**What the literature directly shows.** The most important direct empirical pattern is that AI often benefits less-experienced and lower-performing workers more than top performers ([Brynjolfsson et al., 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [BCG, 2023](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai); [Cui et al., 2025](https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf)). That means AI can flatten some of the advantage previously held by workers whose value came from possessing routine expertise or simply being able to produce first drafts faster. At the same time, [Deloitte’s future-of-work page](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/about/future-of-work-ai.html) argues that coaching, process redesign, creativity, and human judgment become more important even as some managerial layers shrink. [Deloitte’s 2026 high-performing-teams study](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html) adds that high-performing AI teams depend heavily on trust, autonomy, emotional intelligence, and apprenticeship cultures. [Deloitte’s tech-talent article](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/tech-talent-ai-strategy.html) also emphasizes the continuing scarcity of specialized architecture, security, and advanced technical talent. [Gartner’s 2025 survey](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-10-20-gartner-survey-finds-all-it-work-will-involve-ai-by-2030-organizations-must-navigate-ai-readiness-and-human-readiness-to-find-capture-and-sustain-value) supports the same basic direction: most future IT work is expected to be AI-augmented human work.

**What market commentary adds.** [Source Global Research’s 2026 outlook](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth) suggests that AI-based research may replace the bottom layer of the traditional professional-services pyramid, which in turn could make higher-layer expertise more visible and economically important. That aligns neatly with [David Maister’s leverage model](https://davidmaister.com/articles/16/2/): if the grinder layer thins, the firm has to rethink how it develops future minders and finders.

**Reasonable inference for the book.** The book can argue, with reasonable caution, that the strategically valuable employee in an AI-rich consulting firm is increasingly likely to be someone who combines:
- client and domain context,
- architecture or end-to-end workflow judgment,
- exception handling and quality assurance,
- change leadership and stakeholder trust,
- the ability to teach, coach, and codify work for others.

That is partly an inference rather than a directly proven conclusion. But it is a well-grounded inference from the convergence of skill-compression evidence, human-capability research, and consulting-market commentary.

**Source-backed takeaways.**

- AI is likely to reduce the scarcity of some routine expertise while raising the value of context, judgment, and orchestration ([Brynjolfsson et al., 2023](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161); [BCG, 2023](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/how-people-create-and-destroy-value-with-gen-ai)).
- High-performing AI teams appear to need better human systems, not fewer human systems ([Deloitte, 2026](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/about/press-room/high-performing-teams.html); [McKinsey Superagency](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work)).
- Specialized technical talent remains scarce even under strong AI adoption ([Deloitte tech talent churn](https://www.deloitte.com/us/en/services/consulting/articles/tech-talent-ai-strategy.html)).
- The junior layer of the pyramid may shrink, but the need to develop future senior talent does not disappear ([Maister](https://davidmaister.com/articles/16/2/); [Source, 2026](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/2026-teetering-between-high-and-low-growth)).



## 5. Counterarguments and Tensions

1. **AI gains may be real at the task level but weaker than headlines suggest at the firm or economy level.**
   - **Who argues it:** [Daron Acemoglu](https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487), [Humlum and Vestergaard](https://www.nber.org/papers/w33777), and [METR](https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/).
   - **Evidence:** Acemoglu argues that the easiest tasks are automated first and that near-term macro gains may be modest. Humlum and Vestergaard find small early labor-market effects in Danish data. METR finds that AI can slow experienced developers in realistic codebase work.
   - **Implication for the thesis:** The book should avoid claiming that AI has already made software delivery uniformly cheap. It is safer to argue that AI is putting pressure on **specific portions** of delivery economics.

2. **Consulting may gain more than it loses if orchestration, change, and governance become more valuable.**
   - **Who argues it:** [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/), [Accenture](https://investor.accenture.com/~/media/Files/A/accenture-v4/investors/home/accenture-2025-letter-to-shareholders.pdf), [PwC](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/about-us/newsroom/press-releases/pwc-one.html), and [Source Global Research](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/market-update/will-the-efficiency-gains-from-ai-destroy-the-professional-services-industry-probably-not).
   - **Evidence:** These sources consistently say AI speeds delivery and pressures generic hours, but they do not predict consulting extinction. Instead, they emphasize higher-value services, platforms, and transformation work.
   - **Implication for the thesis:** The book should position itself as a **value migration** thesis, not a “death of consulting” thesis.

3. **Some labor reduction may be rational and even necessary.**
   - **Who argues it:** Not usually as formal theory, but it is implicit in employer surveys and in cases like [Klarna’s 2024 AI headcount story](https://www.reuters.com/technology/artificial-intelligence/swedens-klarna-says-ai-chatbots-help-shrink-headcount-2024-08-27/).
   - **Evidence:** Some firms are overstaffed, some delivery layers are redundant, and AI can clearly eliminate certain low-complexity tasks.
   - **Implication for the thesis:** The argument should be against **blunt or poorly targeted cuts**, not all cuts. The book will be stronger if it distinguishes strategic pruning from capability liquidation.

4. **AI can codify some tacit knowledge and reduce continuity risk.**
   - **Who argues it:** [McKinsey’s 2025 innovation article](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-next-innovation-revolution-powered-by-ai) and, implicitly, many enterprise knowledge-management advocates.
   - **Evidence:** AI can summarize meetings, extract patterns from communications, generate reusable playbooks, and make some organizational knowledge more portable.
   - **Implication for the thesis:** The book should acknowledge that AI can preserve parts of tacit knowledge. The sharper claim is that **relational, political, and deeply contextual knowledge remains harder to codify** than process artifacts.

5. **Clients may pay more, not less, for AI-enabled consulting if it improves speed and outcomes.**
   - **Who argues it:** [Source Global Research (2024)](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/our-most-viewed-charts-of-2024-how-have-things-changed) and [Everest Group (2025)](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/).
   - **Evidence:** Source reports substantial client willingness to pay more when firms use AI-enabled tools well, and Everest reports buyer interest in value-driven or gain-sharing pricing.
   - **Implication for the thesis:** Margin compression is not automatic. Firms that can prove value may defend or even expand margins while firms that sell generic labor hours are squeezed.

6. **The organizational-memory argument is compelling, but the AI-specific evidence is still thin.**
   - **Who argues it:** This is a limitation of the literature rather than a rival theory.
   - **Evidence:** The strongest support for knowledge loss comes from downsizing, turnover, and service-performance research ([Galan](https://www.sciencedirect.com/org/science/article/pii/S0969647423000551); [Hong](https://academic.oup.com/jpart/article-abstract/35/4/434/8162522); [Subramony and Holtom](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1094670512452792)), not from direct studies of AI-led consulting layoffs.
   - **Implication for the thesis:** The book should present this part as a **high-confidence strategic inference** rather than as a fully proven empirical law.

### Where the thesis needs nuance

- Replace “AI commoditizes software delivery” with “AI commoditizes **routine, well-specified slices** of software delivery and adjacent knowledge work.”
- Replace “layoffs destroy growth” with “**indiscriminate** layoffs can damage tacit knowledge, continuity, and the apprenticeship pipeline that support long-term growth.”
- Replace “consulting becomes less valuable” with “the value of consulting **migrates** from labor hours to orchestration, platforms, outcomes, and human-plus-AI transformation.”
- Be explicit that the strongest evidence is a blend of **direct empirical findings** and **careful strategic inference**.

## 6. Implications for Book Positioning

### What this book can say that existing thought leadership does not say clearly

Most existing sources do one of four things:

1. They show that AI raises productivity in some tasks.
2. They debate replacement versus augmentation.
3. They describe consulting business-model change.
4. They study turnover, knowledge loss, or downsizing.

Very few sources connect all four into a single management argument for professional-services firms. The book’s strongest differentiated move is to say:

> In an AI era, the critical management question is not just how much labor can be removed, but which human capabilities must be preserved and amplified if productivity gains are supposed to turn into growth.

That is not yet a crowded line.

### Which claims feel crowded

- “AI boosts productivity.”
- “AI changes work.”
- “Consulting firms must adopt AI.”
- “Time-based billing is under pressure.”
- “Knowledge workers will be augmented by AI.”

These claims are true enough, but they are already heavily covered by [McKinsey](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai), [PwC](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/issues/artificial-intelligence/job-barometer/2025/report.pdf), [BCG](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/ai-at-work-momentum-builds-but-gaps-remain), [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com/blogs/the-future-of-ai-consulting-services-is-disruptively-bright/), and [HFS](https://www.hfsresearch.com/research/services-as-software-non-linear-growth/).

### Which claims feel fresh

- **AI changes the retention calculus inside consulting firms.**
- **The hidden cost of AI-era efficiency programs is the loss of tacit client, delivery, and apprenticeship capital.**
- **The real strategic choice is whether productivity gains fund cost takeout or growth capacity.**
- **The firms that win will automate the repeatable shell of delivery while protecting and upgrading the tacit core.**
- **The future premium in consulting is likely to sit at the intersection of AI, workflow redesign, governance, and human capability transfer.**

### Recommended positioning angles

- **From labor arbitrage to growth arbitrage.**
- **The post-billable-hour consulting model.**
- **Automate the routine shell; protect the tacit core.**
- **Do not liquidate the human capital you need to compound.**
- **Why the bottom of the pyramid is under pressure—but judgment, architecture, and apprenticeship still matter.**

### Recommended audiences

- CEOs, COOs, CFOs, CHROs, and managing partners of consulting and IT-services firms.
- Practice leaders inside firms that still depend heavily on software delivery, implementation, managed services, or staff augmentation economics.
- Private-equity operators evaluating services portfolios exposed to AI margin compression.
- Enterprise buyers of consulting who need a new lens for evaluating provider value.
- Strategy, transformation, and talent leaders trying to decide whether AI savings should become cuts, reinvestment, or both.

## 8. Annotated Bibliography

The entries below prioritize the sources most likely to seed the project’s long-term research base.

1. Maister, David H. (1993). *Managing the Professional Service Firm*. Free Press. https://davidmaister.com/books.mtpsf/

   This is the foundational text for the economics and people model of professional-service firms. Its enduring value for this project is the leverage logic: firms profit by structuring work across juniors, managers, and seniors while simultaneously running an apprenticeship pipeline.

   **Use in book:** Use in the opening chapters to explain what AI is destabilizing: not just tasks, but the classic economics and developmental logic of the consulting pyramid.

2. Peng, Sida, et al. (2023). *The Impact of AI on Developer Productivity: Evidence from GitHub Copilot*. arXiv. https://arxiv.org/abs/2302.06590

   This controlled experiment is one of the clearest early pieces of evidence that generative AI can materially speed up coding work. It is best used as proof that AI can shrink effort on bounded programming tasks, not as proof about all software engineering work.

   **Use in book:** Use when establishing the baseline claim that AI is compressing the cost of some code production.

3. Cui, Kevin, Mert Demirer, et al. (2025). *The Effects of Generative AI on High-Skilled Work: Evidence from Three Field Experiments with Software Developers*. Working paper. https://demirermert.github.io/Papers/Demirer_AI_productivity.pdf

   This is arguably the strongest direct evidence for the book’s argument because it studies real developers inside Microsoft, Accenture, and a Fortune 100 company. It shows meaningful output gains and heterogeneous effects, making it ideal for a nuanced claim about which delivery work becomes cheaper fastest.

   **Use in book:** Use as the anchor source for the claim that AI is changing the economics of software-heavy consulting delivery.

4. METR. (2025, July 10). *Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity*. https://metr.org/blog/2025-07-10-early-2025-ai-experienced-os-dev-study/

   This study is the best recent antidote to overclaiming. It shows that experienced developers working in realistic, high-context environments may actually be slower with contemporary AI tools, which sharply improves the thesis by preventing it from becoming too broad.

   **Use in book:** Use in the counterargument chapter and anywhere the book needs to distinguish routine code generation from deep system work.

5. Brynjolfsson, Erik, Danielle Li, and Lindsey R. Raymond. (2023). *Generative AI at Work*. NBER Working Paper No. 31161. https://www.nber.org/papers/w31161

   This paper provides strong field evidence for augmentation, especially for lower-skilled workers, and also reports lower attrition among AI-enabled workers. It is central to the idea that AI changes who becomes valuable and can raise the floor more than the ceiling.

   **Use in book:** Use to support the argument that augmentation and skill compression may matter more than simple labor substitution.

6. Brynjolfsson, Erik, Daniel Rock, and Chad Syverson. (2021). *The Productivity J-Curve: How Intangibles Complement General Purpose Technologies*. NBER Working Paper No. 25148. https://www.nber.org/papers/w25148

   Although it predates the generative-AI boom, this is one of the most useful conceptual papers for the book. Its core point—that general-purpose technologies require complementary organizational investment before full value appears—maps directly onto the claim that AI productivity should be redirected into redesign and growth.

   **Use in book:** Use to justify the book’s strategic stance that cutting labor too quickly can leave the larger payoff unrealized.

7. Autor, David. (2024). *Applying AI to Rebuild Middle Class Jobs*. NBER Working Paper No. 32140. https://www.nber.org/papers/w32140

   Autor offers the clearest labor-economics case that AI can broaden expertise instead of merely automating it away. This source is especially useful for elevating the book beyond a narrow efficiency narrative and grounding the pro-augmentation case in serious scholarship.

   **Use in book:** Use in chapters arguing for employee empowerment, redesign, and capability broadening.

8. Acemoglu, Daron. (2024). *The Simple Macroeconomics of AI*. NBER Working Paper No. 32487. https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487

   Acemoglu provides a rigorous counterweight to sweeping AI claims, arguing that hard tasks and slow organizational adjustment may keep macro gains modest in the near term. This is essential for keeping the book skeptical and analytically credible.

   **Use in book:** Use to qualify the magnitude and timing of the thesis, especially in discussions of near-term industry disruption.

9. Humlum, Anders, and Emilie Vestergaard. (2025). *Early Labor Market Transformation under Generative AI*. NBER Working Paper No. 33777. https://www.nber.org/papers/w33777

   This paper helps separate sensational narratives from early labor-market evidence. Its findings suggest that strong task-level gains do not automatically translate into immediate economy-wide reductions in hours or earnings.

   **Use in book:** Use to caution readers that the consulting-model shift may precede visible aggregate labor-market collapse.

10. Dell’Acqua, Fabrizio, et al. (2023). *Navigating the Jagged Technological Frontier: Field Experimental Evidence of the Effects of Artificial Intelligence on Knowledge Worker Productivity and Quality*. Harvard Business School Working Paper. https://www.hbs.edu/faculty/Pages/item.aspx?num=64700

   This is one of the best sources for explaining why AI creates both value and failure. The “jagged frontier” concept is particularly useful for identifying which consulting tasks are most vulnerable to commoditization and which remain dependent on human judgment.

   **Use in book:** Use to structure the book’s distinction between automatable delivery work and still-scarce transformation work.

11. McKinsey & Company. (2025, January 28). *Superagency in the Workplace: Empowering People to Unlock AI’s Full Potential at Work*. https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/tech-and-ai/our-insights/superagency-in-the-workplace-empowering-people-to-unlock-ais-full-potential-at-work

   This report is a strong source on the organizational bottlenecks to AI value, especially leadership behavior, workflow redesign, and employee sentiment. It is also useful because it directly pushes against the idea that employees are the main obstacle.

   **Use in book:** Use in chapters on growth from productivity and on what distinguishes high-performing AI adopters.

## 9. Research Gaps

### What still needs firsthand reporting, interviews, or proprietary evidence

- **Consulting-firm unit economics.** Public commentary strongly suggests pricing pressure and model change, but the book would be much stronger with proprietary data on utilization, revenue per employee, margin by engagement type, junior-to-senior staffing ratios, and the share of work sold on fixed-fee, outcome-based, or gain-sharing terms.
- **Role-level AI impact inside consulting firms.** Existing evidence is strong on software developers and knowledge workers in general, but thinner on the specific roles that matter most in consulting firms: analysts, associates, engagement managers, architects, PMO leaders, change managers, and client partners.
- **Client willingness to pay.** Analyst commentary suggests clients may pay more for AI-enabled consulting when value is clear ([Source, 2024](https://sourceglobalresearch.com/blog-post/our-most-viewed-charts-of-2024-how-have-things-changed); [Everest, 2025](https://www.everestgrp.com/report/egr-2025-71-r-6956/)), but the book would benefit from direct interviews with enterprise buyers on what they will and will not pay for.
- **Apprenticeship pipeline risk.** The book’s most distinctive claim may be that AI reduces the need for junior labor faster than it reduces the need for future senior talent. That is conceptually powerful, but it still needs stronger direct evidence from firms that are redesigning analyst and manager career paths.
- **Transformation success stories.** There is plenty of commentary about AI-driven reinvention, but fewer detailed, transparent case studies showing consulting firms or enterprise functions that redirected AI productivity into growth rather than cuts.

### Which claims are currently more conceptual than proven

- That AI-era layoffs in consulting will systematically reduce long-term firm growth.
- That the “bottom of the pyramid” will shrink enough to force a wholesale redesign of the consulting career ladder across the industry.
- That the most valuable retained employees will be domain integrators, architects, and teachers rather than traditional utilization-driven middle layers. This is a strong inference, but still an inference.
- That AI-enabled firms will reliably achieve better margins through outcome pricing rather than simply lower realized fees per unit of labor.

### Which future chapters would need stronger evidence

- **A chapter on the collapse of the junior-delivery pyramid** would need interviews with firm leaders and internal staffing data.
- **A chapter on tacit knowledge and continuity** would benefit from case studies of projects damaged by layoffs, offshoring, or automation-heavy redesign.
- **A chapter on who to retain** would need role-by-role analysis, not just high-level rhetoric about “human judgment.”
- **A chapter on growth from productivity** would need empirical examples of firms that used AI gains to increase revenue, wallet share, or speed to market rather than only reduce labor cost.
- **A chapter on the future consulting business model** would be stronger with contract examples showing the shift from time-based billing to subscription, outcome-based, or shared-risk structures.
